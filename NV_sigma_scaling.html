Positional Accuracy of NV Centers:
From a Single Emitter to the General M-Emitter Problem

A summary of σ scaling and the role of superresolution — with derivations

The Single-Emitter Baseline

The simplest case is a single, isolated NV center imaged on a diffraction-limited confocal microscope. The PSF has a Gaussian profile of width $\spsf$ (approximately 125 nm for NA = 1.4 at 637 nm). Collecting $N$ photons, the Cramér–Rao bound gives the fundamental limit on positional accuracy:

$$\sigma_x \;\geq\; \frac{\spsf}{\sqrt{N}} \tag{1}$$
Thompson–Ober–Webb (2002)

Goal: show that for $N$ photons drawn from a Gaussian PSF, the best possible position uncertainty is $\spsf/\sqrt{N}$.

Step 1 — probability of a single photon

Each photon lands at detector position $x$ drawn from the PSF centered at the true NV position $x_0$:

$$p(x \mid x_0) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\,\spsf}\exp\!\left(-\frac{(x-x_0)^2}{2\spsf^2}\right)$$

This is just the normalized Gaussian — the probability density of detecting a photon at position $x$.

Step 2 — the score (derivative of log-likelihood)

Take the natural log:

$$\ln p = -\tfrac{1}{2}\ln(2\pi\spsf^2) - \frac{(x-x_0)^2}{2\spsf^2}$$

Differentiate with respect to $x_0$ (the parameter we want to estimate):

$$\frac{\partial \ln p}{\partial x_0} = \frac{x - x_0}{\spsf^2}$$

This is called the score. It measures how sensitively the log-likelihood responds to a shift in $x_0$ for a single observed photon landing at $x$.

Step 3 — Fisher information for one photon

The Fisher information is the expected squared score:

$$I_1 = \E\!\left[\left(\frac{x-x_0}{\spsf^2}\right)^{\!2}\right] = \frac{1}{\spsf^4}\,\E\!\left[(x-x_0)^2\right]$$

Now $\E[(x-x_0)^2]$ is just the variance of the Gaussian distribution, which is exactly $\spsf^2$. Therefore:

$$I_1 = \frac{\spsf^2}{\spsf^4} = \frac{1}{\spsf^2}$$

Wider PSF $\Rightarrow$ smaller Fisher information per photon $\Rightarrow$ each photon tells you less about $x_0$. This makes intuitive sense.

Step 4 — Fisher information for $N$ independent photons

The $N$ photons are detected independently, so the joint log-likelihood is a sum of individual log-likelihoods. Because the score of a sum is the sum of scores, and because independent zero-mean random variables have additive variances:

$$I_N = N \cdot I_1 = \frac{N}{\spsf^2}$$

Fisher information is additive over independent measurements. This is the key reason $N$ appears in the denominator.

Step 5 — apply the Cramér–Rao bound

The CRB states that for any unbiased estimator $\hat x_0$:

$$\Var(\hat x_0) \geq \frac{1}{I_N} = \frac{\spsf^2}{N}$$

Taking the square root gives Eq. (1). The bound is achieved exactly by the sample mean $\hat x_0 = \frac{1}{N}\sum_k x_k$, whose variance is $\spsf^2/N$ by direct calculation. So the CRB is tight here — the $1/\sqrt{N}$ scaling is not just a lower bound, it is the exact answer for the optimal estimator.

Resolution and positional accuracy are distinct. $\spsf$ sets the resolution (minimum resolvable separation); $\sigma_x$ sets the positional accuracy (uncertainty on a single known emitter's location). The first is fixed by the optics; the second shrinks as $1/\sqrt{N}$.

No superresolution is needed for a single isolated emitter. A standard confocal ($\spsf = 250$ nm) with $N = 10^6$ photons achieves $\sigma_x \approx 0.25$ nm — far below the diffraction limit.

Two NV Centers with Known Separation

Suppose two NV centers are separated by $d$, and $d$ is known precisely. We wish to recover $x_1$ and $x_2$.

When $d \gg \spsf$ the PSFs barely overlap. Each emitter is effectively isolated and the single-emitter result applies to each with half the photon budget:

$$\sigma_{x_1},\,\sigma_{x_2} \;\approx\; \frac{\spsf}{\sqrt{N/2}} \tag{2a}$$

Each NV emits $N/2$ photons on average (assuming equal brightness). The Eq. (1) result applies to each independently with its own photon count. Plugging $N \to N/2$ gives $\spsf/\sqrt{N/2} = \spsf\sqrt{2}/\sqrt{N}$ — a $\sqrt{2}$ penalty relative to a single emitter using the full budget. This penalty vanishes if you can separate the two photon streams (e.g., by ODMR spin state), in which case each emitter effectively has its own independent camera.

When $d \ll \spsf$ the PSFs strongly overlap. Knowing $d$ still saves us via a parameter change. Write:

$$x_1 = \bar x - \tfrac{d}{2}, \qquad x_2 = \bar x + \tfrac{d}{2}, \qquad \bar x = \tfrac{x_1+x_2}{2}$$

The centroid $\bar x$ is always well-determined (it shifts the combined PSF). So the uncertainty in each individual position is:

$$\sigma_{x_1}^2 \;=\; \sxbar^2 + \tfrac{1}{4}\sd^2 \tag{2b}$$

We have a deterministic relation $x_1 = \bar x - d/2$. Standard error propagation (the delta method) says: if $z = f(u, v)$, then $\sigma_z^2 = (\partial f/\partial u)^2 \sigma_u^2 + (\partial f/\partial v)^2 \sigma_v^2 + 2(\partial f/\partial u)(\partial f/\partial v)\,\mathrm{Cov}(u,v)$.

Here $f(\bar x, d) = \bar x - d/2$, so $\partial f/\partial \bar x = 1$ and $\partial f/\partial d = -1/2$. Assuming $\bar x$ and $d$ are estimated independently (their estimators are uncorrelated — this follows from the Fisher information matrix being block-diagonal between the symmetric and antisymmetric combinations for equal-brightness emitters):

$$\sigma_{x_1}^2 = 1^2 \cdot \sxbar^2 + \left(-\tfrac{1}{2}\right)^2 \sd^2 = \sxbar^2 + \tfrac{1}{4}\sd^2$$

If $d$ is known exactly then $\sd = 0$ and we get $\sigma_{x_1} = \sxbar \approx \spsf/\sqrt{N}$ — the single-emitter result is fully recovered. Known separation costs nothing.

Two NV Centers with Unknown Separation: the Rayleigh Curse

Now $d$ is unknown. We must estimate $(x_1, x_2)$ jointly from the image — equivalently, we estimate $(\bar x, d)$. The centroid remains easy, but $d$ becomes the bottleneck.

Fisher information for separation collapses near $d=0$

$$I(d) \;\propto\; \left(\frac{d}{\spsf}\right)^{\!2} \quad \text{as } d\to 0 \tag{3a}$$

The combined intensity profile for two equal emitters at $\pm d/2$ is:

$$p(x \mid d) = \frac{1}{2}\,G(x+d/2) + \frac{1}{2}\,G(x-d/2)$$

where $G(u) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\spsf}e^{-u^2/2\spsf^2}$. The Fisher information for $d$ is:

$$I(d) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{\left[\partial p/\partial d\right]^2}{p(x\mid d)}\,dx$$
Compute $\partial p / \partial d$
$$\frac{\partial p}{\partial d} = \frac{1}{2}\left[-\frac{1}{2}G'(x+d/2) + \frac{1}{2}G'(x-d/2)\right]$$

where $G'(u) = -\frac{u}{\spsf^2}G(u)$. At $d = 0$ both terms are $G'(x)$ with opposite signs, so:

$$\left.\frac{\partial p}{\partial d}\right|_{d=0} = \frac{1}{2}\left[-\frac{1}{2}G'(x) + \frac{1}{2}G'(x)\right] = 0$$

The derivative vanishes identically at $d=0$ — this is the symmetry argument. At $d=0$ the two emitters are indistinguishable; pulling them apart to first order produces no change in the image.

Taylor expand for small $d$

Since the first-order term is zero, expand to second order in $d$:

$$\frac{\partial p}{\partial d} \approx \frac{d}{4}\left[G''(x-d/2) + G''(x+d/2)\right]\Big|_{d\to 0} \propto d \cdot G''(x)$$

So $\partial p/\partial d \propto d$ for small $d$. Squaring and integrating:

$$I(d) \propto d^2 \int \frac{[G''(x)]^2}{G(x)}\,dx \propto \frac{d^2}{\spsf^2}$$

The integral $\int [G''(x)]^2/G(x)\,dx$ is finite and evaluates to $3/(2\spsf^4)$ for a Gaussian (it is the Fisher information for the width parameter). The key result is that $I(d) \propto d^2/\spsf^2$, going to zero quadratically as $d\to 0$.

Because $I(d) \propto d^2$, the Cramér–Rao bound on $d$ diverges as $d \to 0$:

$$\sd \;\geq\; \frac{\spsf^2}{d\,\sqrt{N}} \tag{3b}$$

Write $I_1(d) = c\,d^2/\spsf^2$ for some numerical constant $c$ of order 1. For $N$ photons, total Fisher information is $I_N(d) = N \cdot c\, d^2/\spsf^2$. The CRB gives:

$$\sd^2 \geq \frac{1}{I_N(d)} = \frac{\spsf^2}{N c d^2}$$

Taking the square root and absorbing $c$ into the proportionality:

$$\sd \gtrsim \frac{\spsf^2}{d\sqrt{N}}$$

Note the structure: the $1/\sqrt{N}$ from the CRB is now multiplied by $\spsf^2/d$, which grows without bound as $d\to 0$. More photons still help (they enter as $1/\sqrt{N}$) but you are fighting an increasingly bad geometric prefactor.

Individual positions inherit the curse

Substituting $\sxbar \approx \spsf/\sqrt{N}$ and $\sd \approx \spsf^2/(d\sqrt{N})$ into Eq. (2b):

$$\sigma_{x_1}^2 \approx \frac{\spsf^2}{N} + \frac{1}{4}\cdot\frac{\spsf^4}{d^2 N} = \frac{\spsf^2}{N}\left(1 + \frac{\spsf^2}{4d^2}\right)$$

When $d \ll \spsf$ the second term dominates:

$$\sigma_{x_1} \;\approx\; \frac{\spsf^2}{2d\,\sqrt{N}} \tag{3c}$$

Start from Eq. (2b): $\sigma_{x_1}^2 = \sxbar^2 + \frac{1}{4}\sd^2$.

We know $\sxbar \approx \spsf/\sqrt{N}$ (centroid of the combined PSF, same as single emitter). And from Eq. (3b), $\sd \approx \spsf^2/(d\sqrt{N})$. Substituting:

$$\sigma_{x_1}^2 \approx \frac{\spsf^2}{N} + \frac{1}{4}\cdot\frac{\spsf^4}{d^2 N}$$

Factor out $\spsf^2/N$:

$$\sigma_{x_1}^2 \approx \frac{\spsf^2}{N}\left[1 + \frac{\spsf^2}{4d^2}\right]$$

When $d \ll \spsf$ (specifically $d \ll \spsf/2$), the second term $\spsf^2/(4d^2) \gg 1$ dominates, so we drop the first term:

$$\sigma_{x_1}^2 \approx \frac{\spsf^4}{4d^2 N}$$

Taking the square root gives Eq. (3c). The factor of $\spsf/(2d)$ is how much worse you are compared to a single isolated emitter — for $d = 10$ nm and $\spsf = 125$ nm this factor is about 6, meaning you need $6^2 = 36\times$ more photons just to match the isolated-emitter performance.

$M$ NV Centers with Known $M$: the Combinatorial Catastrophe

Density penalty

For $M$ emitters uniformly distributed in a disk of radius $\spsf$, the mean nearest-neighbor separation is:

$$\bar d \;\sim\; \frac{\spsf}{\sqrt{M}} \tag{4a}$$

We have $M$ points distributed uniformly in a disk of area $A = \pi\spsf^2$, giving number density $\rho = M/A = M/(\pi\spsf^2)$.

For a uniform 2D Poisson point process with density $\rho$, the probability that the nearest neighbor is further than distance $r$ is the probability of finding zero other points within a disk of radius $r$:

$$P(\text{nearest neighbor} > r) = e^{-\rho \pi r^2}$$

This is just the Poisson zero-count probability. The mean nearest-neighbor distance is:

$$\E[r_{\min}] = \int_0^\infty P(r_{\min} > r)\,dr = \int_0^\infty e^{-\rho\pi r^2}\,dr = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{\rho}}$$

The integral is a Gaussian: $\int_0^\infty e^{-\alpha r^2}dr = \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{\pi/\alpha}$, so with $\alpha = \rho\pi$ we get $\frac{1}{2}\sqrt{1/\rho}$.

Substituting $\rho = M/(\pi\spsf^2)$:

$$\bar d = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{M/(\pi\spsf^2)}} = \frac{\spsf}{2}\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{M}} \sim \frac{\spsf}{\sqrt{M}}$$

absorbing the numerical prefactor $\sqrt{\pi}/2 \approx 0.89$ into the $\sim$ sign, since we only care about the scaling.

Substituting $\bar d \sim \spsf/\sqrt{M}$ into Eq. (3c):

$$\sx \;\sim\; \frac{\spsf\,\sqrt{M}}{\sqrt{N}} \tag{4b}$$

From Eq. (3c), the individual position uncertainty for two nearby emitters separated by $d$ is $\sigma \approx \spsf^2/(2d\sqrt{N})$. Replace $d$ with the typical nearest-neighbor separation $\bar d \sim \spsf/\sqrt{M}$:

$$\sx \sim \frac{\spsf^2}{2\,(\spsf/\sqrt{M})\,\sqrt{N}} = \frac{\spsf^2 \sqrt{M}}{2\,\spsf\,\sqrt{N}} = \frac{\spsf\sqrt{M}}{2\sqrt{N}}$$

Dropping the factor of 2 (order-of-magnitude scaling):

$$\sx \sim \frac{\spsf\sqrt{M}}{\sqrt{N}}$$

The $\sqrt{M}$ growth makes sense: more emitters $\Rightarrow$ smaller $\bar d$ $\Rightarrow$ stronger Rayleigh curse on each pair $\Rightarrow$ each emitter is harder to localize. Note that the $\sqrt{M}$ factor means you need $M$ times more photons to maintain the same $\sigma_x$ as $M$ grows — but since the total photon budget is also shared over $M$ emitters, this is a genuinely bad situation.

Permutation explosion and Fisher matrix ill-conditioning

With $M$ emitters the likelihood surface has $M!$ peaks. The Fisher information matrix becomes ill-conditioned; its smallest eigenvalue scales as:

$$\lambda_{\min} \;\propto\; \left(\frac{\bar d}{\spsf}\right)^{2(M-1)} N \tag{4c}$$

The Fisher information matrix $\mathbf{F}$ for the $2M$ position parameters has entries:

$$F_{ij} = N\int \frac{(\partial p/\partial\theta_i)(\partial p/\partial\theta_j)}{p}\,dx$$

where $\theta_i$ are the $2M$ position coordinates. Consider the eigenvector corresponding to the smallest eigenvalue: it is the mode of parameter variation to which the image is least sensitive. For $M$ emitters this is a collective rearrangement that moves all emitters while barely changing the total intensity profile.

Physically, the worst case is the "accordion mode" where emitters move simultaneously in a pattern that is nearly invisible at the detector. Each pairwise Rayleigh curse contributes a factor of $(d/\spsf)^2$ to the determinant of the Fisher matrix. With $M-1$ independent separations (one centroid + $M-1$ relative positions), the determinant scales as:

$$\det(\mathbf{F}) \propto N^{2M} \left(\frac{\bar d}{\spsf}\right)^{2(M-1)}$$

The smallest eigenvalue is roughly the determinant divided by the product of all other eigenvalues. The centroid eigenvalue stays $\sim N/\spsf^2$ regardless of $d$. After dividing out the $M-1$ "easy" eigenvalues (which also scale as $N/\spsf^2$), the remaining smallest one scales as:

$$\lambda_{\min} \propto N\left(\frac{\bar d}{\spsf}\right)^{2(M-1)}$$

This goes to zero exponentially fast in $M$ (since $\bar d/\spsf = 1/\sqrt{M} < 1$), explaining why the problem becomes rapidly intractable as $M$ grows.

The corresponding worst-case position uncertainty is:

$$\sigma_{\max} \;\sim\; \frac{\spsf^M}{\bar d^{\,M-1}\,\sqrt{N}} \tag{4d}$$

The CRB for the worst-case direction in parameter space is $\sigma_{\max}^2 \geq 1/\lambda_{\min}$. Using Eq. (4c) with $\bar d = \spsf/\sqrt{M}$:

$$\sigma_{\max}^2 \sim \frac{1}{N} \cdot \left(\frac{\spsf}{\bar d}\right)^{2(M-1)} = \frac{\spsf^{2(M-1)}}{\bar d^{\,2(M-1)}\,N}$$

Taking the square root:

$$\sigma_{\max} \sim \frac{\spsf^{M-1}}{\bar d^{\,M-1}\,\sqrt{N}} = \frac{\spsf^{M-1}\,\spsf}{\bar d^{\,M-1}\,\sqrt{N}} = \frac{\spsf^{M}}{\bar d^{\,M-1}\,\sqrt{N}}$$

This is exponential in $M$: substituting $\bar d = \spsf/\sqrt{M}$ gives $\sigma_{\max} \sim \spsf\,M^{(M-1)/2}/\sqrt{N}$, which grows faster than any polynomial in $M$.

The General Case: $M$ Unknown

The most honest version of the problem: $M$ is unknown. We must simultaneously select the model (how many emitters?) and estimate parameters.

Three regimes of $N$

Low-$N$ regime. Model selection error dominates — wrongly identifying $M$ introduces a bias that does not shrink as $1/\sqrt{N}$.

Intermediate regime. The correct $M^*$ is identified with high probability above a threshold:

$$N_{\mathrm{threshold}} \;\sim\; \left(\frac{\spsf}{d_{\min}}\right)^{2M^*} \tag{5a}$$

To correctly identify $M^*$ vs $M^*-1$ emitters, the likelihood ratio between the two models must be statistically distinguishable from noise. The log-likelihood difference between the true model ($M^*$ emitters) and the simpler model ($M^*-1$ emitters) grows as the Fisher information for the "extra" separation that the simpler model misses.

From Eq. (3a), the Fisher information for one separation at distance $d$ is $\propto d^2/\spsf^2$. The likelihood ratio test statistic for detecting one extra emitter at separation $d$ from the existing cluster scales as $N \cdot (d/\spsf)^2$. We can correctly detect the extra emitter when this exceeds a threshold of order 1:

$$N \cdot \frac{d_{\min}^2}{\spsf^2} \gtrsim 1 \quad\Rightarrow\quad N \gtrsim \frac{\spsf^2}{d_{\min}^2}$$

But this argument must be applied $M^*-1$ times — once for each successive emitter being added to the model. Each additional emitter contributes another factor of $(\spsf/d_{\min})^2$ to the threshold (because each new separation has its own Rayleigh curse). Multiplying $M^*-1$ factors together:

$$N_{\mathrm{threshold}} \sim \left(\frac{\spsf}{d_{\min}}\right)^{2(M^*-1)} \sim \left(\frac{\spsf}{d_{\min}}\right)^{2M^*}$$

absorbing the $-1$ in the exponent into the $\sim$. This exponential growth in $M^*$ is why model selection becomes astronomically hard for densely packed emitters: for $d_{\min} = \spsf/10$ and $M^* = 5$ you need $10^{10}$ photons just to correctly count the emitters, before you even start localizing them.

Combined scaling

$$\sx \;\sim\; \underbrace{\frac{\spsf}{\sqrt{N}}}_{\displaystyle\text{single-emitter}} \times \underbrace{\sqrt{M^*}}_{\displaystyle\text{density}} \times \underbrace{\!\left(\frac{\spsf}{d_{\min}}\right)^{\!M^*-1}}_{\displaystyle\text{Rayleigh (compounded)}} \times \underbrace{g(N,M^*,d_{\min})}_{\displaystyle\text{model selection}} \tag{5b}$$

The four factors each come from a different layer of the problem:

Factor 1: $\spsf/\sqrt{N}$

This is the single-emitter CRB from Eq. (1). It is always present as a floor — you cannot do better than this even in ideal conditions.

Factor 2: $\sqrt{M^*}$

From Eq. (4b). Packing $M^*$ emitters into a diffraction volume reduces the typical nearest-neighbor separation to $\bar d \sim \spsf/\sqrt{M^*}$, which degrades each emitter's localization by $\sqrt{M^*}$ via the Rayleigh curse.

Factor 3: $(\spsf/d_{\min})^{M^*-1}$

From Eq. (4c)–(4d). The Fisher matrix ill-conditioning compounds over all $M^*-1$ independent separations, each contributing a factor of $\spsf/d_{\min}$. This is the dominant term when $M^*$ is large and $d_{\min} \ll \spsf$.

Factor 4: $g(N, M^*, d_{\min})$

The model-selection penalty. When $N < N_{\mathrm{threshold}}$ from Eq. (5a), $g \gg 1$ because you are likely fitting the wrong $M$ and the positional errors have a large systematic component. Once $N \gg N_{\mathrm{threshold}}$, $g \to 1$ and only the first three factors matter. In practice for $M^* \geq 4$ at sub-diffraction densities, $g$ never reaches 1 with realistic photon budgets.

How Superresolution Rescues the Problem

All pathologies are controlled by one quantity: the number of emitters per resolution element,

$$M_{\mathrm{eff}} \;=\; \rho\,\bigl(\seff\bigr)^2 \tag{6a}$$

$\rho$ is the 2D number density of NV centers (emitters per nm²), fixed by sample preparation. $\seff$ is the effective PSF radius of the imaging system, which superresolution can shrink below the diffraction limit. The product $\rho \cdot (\seff)^2$ is a dimensionless number: it counts how many emitters fall within an area of $\seff^2$ — i.e., within one resolution element.

More precisely: the area of a disk of radius $\seff$ is $\pi(\seff)^2$; the expected number of emitters within it is $\rho\pi(\seff)^2$. The formula absorbs the $\pi$ into the $\sim$ notation, since we only care about the scaling.

Why does this control everything? Look back at the scaling results:

  • Eq. (4b): $\sx \sim \spsf\sqrt{M}/\sqrt{N}$ — depends on $M$ within the PSF area, i.e. $M_\mathrm{eff}$.
  • Eq. (4d): $\sigma_\mathrm{max} \sim \spsf^M/(\bar d^{M-1}\sqrt{N})$ — depends on $M_\mathrm{eff}$ through $M$ and $\bar d \sim \spsf/\sqrt{M_\mathrm{eff}}$.
  • Eq. (5a): $N_\mathrm{threshold} \sim (\spsf/d_\mathrm{min})^{2M^*}$ — depends on how many emitters are packed within $\spsf$.

When $M_\mathrm{eff} \leq 1$: $M=1$ within the resolution element, all multi-emitter pathologies vanish ($1! = 1$, Fisher matrix is $1\times 1$ and trivially well-conditioned), and we recover $\sx = \seff/\sqrt{N}$.

Deterministic PSF narrowing (STED, GSD, spin-RESOLFT). Shrink $\seff$ by factor $f$: this reduces $M_\mathrm{eff}$ by $f^2$. The cost is scan time $\propto f^2$ (more pixels for the same field of view).

$$\seff \to \frac{\seff}{f} \;\Rightarrow\; M_\mathrm{eff} \to \frac{M_\mathrm{eff}}{f^2} \tag{6b}$$

From Eq. (6a): $M_\mathrm{eff} = \rho\,(\seff)^2$. If we reduce $\seff$ by a factor $f$ (i.e. $\seff \to \seff/f$), then:

$$M_\mathrm{eff}^{\mathrm{new}} = \rho\,\left(\frac{\seff}{f}\right)^2 = \frac{\rho\,\seff^2}{f^2} = \frac{M_\mathrm{eff}}{f^2}$$

The quadratic dependence on PSF width is because the resolution element is 2D — its area shrinks as the square of the linear dimension. This is why a factor of 10 improvement in resolution (say STED achieving 25 nm instead of 250 nm) gives a factor of 100 reduction in $M_\mathrm{eff}$, which can easily take a sample from $M_\mathrm{eff} \gg 1$ (intractable) to $M_\mathrm{eff} \ll 1$ (trivially solvable).

Stochastic emitter thinning (STORM, single-spin STORM). Keep $\seff \approx 250$ nm but reduce $\rho_\mathrm{eff}$ per frame by switching most emitters off. Each frame has $M_\mathrm{eff} \ll 1$; the full image is reconstructed over many frames at a time cost of $1/(\text{active fraction})$.

In both cases superresolution does not improve the $1/\sqrt{N}$ scaling — it rescues the prefactor by enforcing $M_\mathrm{eff} \leq 1$.
