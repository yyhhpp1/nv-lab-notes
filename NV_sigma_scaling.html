The Single-Emitter Baseline

The simplest case is a single, isolated NV center imaged on a diffraction-limited confocal microscope. The PSF has a Gaussian profile of width $\spsf$ (approximately 125 nm for NA = 1.4 at 637 nm). Collecting $N$ photons, the Cramér–Rao bound gives the fundamental limit on positional accuracy:

$$\sigma_x \;\geq\; \frac{\spsf}{\sqrt{N}} \tag{1}$$
Thompson–Ober–Webb (2002)

Goal: show that for $N$ photons drawn from a Gaussian PSF, the best possible position uncertainty is $\spsf/\sqrt{N}$.

Step 1 — probability of a single photon

Each photon lands at detector position $x$ drawn from the PSF centered at the true NV position $x_0$:

$$p(x \mid x_0) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\,\spsf}\exp\!\left(-\frac{(x-x_0)^2}{2\spsf^2}\right)$$

This is just the normalized Gaussian — the probability density of detecting a photon at position $x$.

Step 2 — the score (derivative of log-likelihood)

Take the natural log:

$$\ln p = -\tfrac{1}{2}\ln(2\pi\spsf^2) - \frac{(x-x_0)^2}{2\spsf^2}$$

Differentiate with respect to $x_0$ (the parameter we want to estimate):

$$\frac{\partial \ln p}{\partial x_0} = \frac{x - x_0}{\spsf^2}$$

This is called the score. It measures how sensitively the log-likelihood responds to a shift in $x_0$ for a single observed photon landing at $x$.

Step 3 — Fisher information for one photon

The Fisher information is the expected squared score:

$$I_1 = \E\!\left[\left(\frac{x-x_0}{\spsf^2}\right)^{\!2}\right] = \frac{1}{\spsf^4}\,\E\!\left[(x-x_0)^2\right]$$

Now $\E[(x-x_0)^2]$ is just the variance of the Gaussian distribution, which is exactly $\spsf^2$. Therefore:

$$I_1 = \frac{\spsf^2}{\spsf^4} = \frac{1}{\spsf^2}$$

Wider PSF $\Rightarrow$ smaller Fisher information per photon $\Rightarrow$ each photon tells you less about $x_0$. This makes intuitive sense.

Step 4 — Fisher information for $N$ independent photons

The $N$ photons are detected independently, so the joint log-likelihood is a sum of individual log-likelihoods. Because the score of a sum is the sum of scores, and because independent zero-mean random variables have additive variances:

$$I_N = N \cdot I_1 = \frac{N}{\spsf^2}$$

Fisher information is additive over independent measurements. This is the key reason $N$ appears in the denominator.

Step 5 — apply the Cramér–Rao bound

The CRB states that for any unbiased estimator $\hat x_0$:

$$\Var(\hat x_0) \geq \frac{1}{I_N} = \frac{\spsf^2}{N}$$

Taking the square root gives Eq. (1). The bound is achieved exactly by the sample mean $\hat x_0 = \frac{1}{N}\sum_k x_k$, whose variance is $\spsf^2/N$ by direct calculation. So the CRB is tight here — the $1/\sqrt{N}$ scaling is not just a lower bound, it is the exact answer for the optimal estimator.

Resolution and positional accuracy are distinct. $\spsf$ sets the resolution (minimum resolvable separation); $\sigma_x$ sets the positional accuracy (uncertainty on a single known emitter's location). The first is fixed by the optics; the second shrinks as $1/\sqrt{N}$.

What $N$ means here. Throughout this note $N$ denotes the total detected PL photons integrated over the entire scan — the relevant quantity for the position CRB. This is distinct from the per-shot count $\alpha = R\,t_R$ used in spin-readout SNR analyses. A single confocal image built from many laser pulses contributes all of those detected photons to $N$.

No superresolution is needed for a single isolated emitter. A standard confocal ($\spsf = 250$ nm) with $N = 10^6$ photons achieves $\sigma_x \approx 0.25$ nm — far below the diffraction limit.

Two NV Centers with Known Separation

Suppose two NV centers are separated by $d$, and $d$ is known precisely. We wish to recover $x_1$ and $x_2$.

When $d \gg \spsf$ the PSFs barely overlap. Each emitter is effectively isolated and the single-emitter result applies to each with half the photon budget:

$$\sigma_{x_i} \;\approx\; \frac{\spsf}{\sqrt{N/2}}, \qquad i = 1,2 \tag{2a}$$

Each NV emits $N/2$ photons on average (assuming equal brightness). The Eq. (1) result applies to each independently with its own photon count. Plugging $N \to N/2$ gives $\spsf/\sqrt{N/2} = \spsf\sqrt{2}/\sqrt{N}$ — a $\sqrt{2}$ penalty relative to a single emitter using the full budget. This penalty vanishes if you can separate the two photon streams (e.g., by ODMR spin state), in which case each emitter effectively has its own independent camera.

When $d \ll \spsf$ the PSFs strongly overlap. Knowing $d$ still saves us via a parameter change. Write:

$$x_1 = \bar x - \tfrac{d}{2}, \qquad x_2 = \bar x + \tfrac{d}{2}, \qquad \bar x = \tfrac{x_1+x_2}{2}$$

The centroid $\bar x$ is always well-determined (it shifts the combined PSF). So the uncertainty in each individual position is:

$$\sigma_{x_1}^2 \;=\; \sxbar^2 + \tfrac{1}{4}\sd^2 \tag{2b}$$

We have a deterministic relation $x_1 = \bar x - d/2$. Standard error propagation (the delta method) says: if $z = f(u, v)$, then $\sigma_z^2 = (\partial f/\partial u)^2 \sigma_u^2 + (\partial f/\partial v)^2 \sigma_v^2 + 2(\partial f/\partial u)(\partial f/\partial v)\,\mathrm{Cov}(u,v)$.

Here $f(\bar x, d) = \bar x - d/2$, so $\partial f/\partial \bar x = 1$ and $\partial f/\partial d = -1/2$. Assuming $\bar x$ and $d$ are estimated independently (their estimators are uncorrelated — this follows from the Fisher information matrix being block-diagonal between the symmetric and antisymmetric combinations for equal-brightness emitters):

$$\sigma_{x_1}^2 = 1^2 \cdot \sxbar^2 + \left(-\tfrac{1}{2}\right)^2 \sd^2 = \sxbar^2 + \tfrac{1}{4}\sd^2$$

If $d$ is known exactly then $\sd = 0$ and we get $\sigma_{x_1} = \sxbar \approx \spsf/\sqrt{N}$ — the single-emitter result is fully recovered. Known separation costs nothing.

Two NV Centers with Unknown Separation: the Rayleigh Curse

Now $d$ is unknown. We must estimate $(x_1, x_2)$ jointly from the image — equivalently, we estimate $(\bar x, d)$. The centroid remains easy, but $d$ becomes the bottleneck.

Fisher information for separation collapses near $d=0$

$$I(d) \;\propto\; \left(\frac{d}{\spsf}\right)^{\!2} \quad \text{as } d\to 0 \tag{3a}$$

The combined intensity profile for two equal emitters at $\pm d/2$ is:

$$p(x \mid d) = \frac{1}{2}\,G(x+d/2) + \frac{1}{2}\,G(x-d/2)$$

where $G(u) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\spsf}e^{-u^2/2\spsf^2}$. The Fisher information for $d$ is:

$$I(d) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{\left[\partial p/\partial d\right]^2}{p(x\mid d)}\,dx$$
Compute $\partial p / \partial d$
$$\frac{\partial p}{\partial d} = \frac{1}{2}\left[-\frac{1}{2}G'(x+d/2) + \frac{1}{2}G'(x-d/2)\right]$$

where $G'(u) = -\frac{u}{\spsf^2}G(u)$. At $d = 0$ both terms are $G'(x)$ with opposite signs, so:

$$\left.\frac{\partial p}{\partial d}\right|_{d=0} = \frac{1}{2}\left[-\frac{1}{2}G'(x) + \frac{1}{2}G'(x)\right] = 0$$

The derivative vanishes identically at $d=0$ — this is the symmetry argument. At $d=0$ the two emitters are indistinguishable; pulling them apart to first order produces no change in the image.

Taylor expand for small $d$

Since the first-order term is zero, expand to second order in $d$:

$$\frac{\partial p}{\partial d} \approx \frac{d}{4}\left[G''(x-d/2) + G''(x+d/2)\right]\Big|_{d\to 0} \propto d \cdot G''(x)$$

So $\partial p/\partial d \propto d$ for small $d$. Squaring and integrating:

$$I(d) \propto d^2 \int \frac{[G''(x)]^2}{G(x)}\,dx \propto \frac{d^2}{\spsf^2}$$

The integral $\int [G''(x)]^2/G(x)\,dx$ is finite and evaluates to $3/(2\spsf^4)$ for a Gaussian (it is the Fisher information for the width parameter). The key result is that $I(d) \propto d^2/\spsf^2$, going to zero quadratically as $d\to 0$.

Because $I(d) \propto d^2$, the Cramér–Rao bound on $d$ diverges as $d \to 0$:

First derived in the imaging context by Helstrom (1976) and elaborated by Ram, Ward & Ober (PNAS 2006). The term "Rayleigh's curse" is due to Tsang, Nair & Lu (PRX 2016), who also showed that spatial-mode demultiplexing (SPADE) can in principle bypass the curse. For photostable emitters like NV centers this direction — engineering the measurement basis rather than the PSF — is an active area.

$$\sd \;\geq\; \frac{\spsf^2}{d\,\sqrt{N}} \tag{3b}$$

Write $I_1(d) = c\,d^2/\spsf^2$ for some numerical constant $c$ of order 1. For $N$ photons, total Fisher information is $I_N(d) = N \cdot c\, d^2/\spsf^2$. The CRB gives:

$$\sd^2 \geq \frac{1}{I_N(d)} = \frac{\spsf^2}{N c d^2}$$

Taking the square root and absorbing $c$ into the proportionality:

$$\sd \gtrsim \frac{\spsf^2}{d\sqrt{N}}$$

Note the structure: the $1/\sqrt{N}$ from the CRB is now multiplied by $\spsf^2/d$, which grows without bound as $d\to 0$. More photons still help (they enter as $1/\sqrt{N}$) but you are fighting an increasingly bad geometric prefactor.

Individual positions inherit the curse

Substituting $\sxbar \approx \spsf/\sqrt{N}$ and $\sd \approx \spsf^2/(d\sqrt{N})$ into Eq. (2b):

$$\sigma_{x_1}^2 \approx \frac{\spsf^2}{N} + \frac{1}{4}\cdot\frac{\spsf^4}{d^2 N} = \frac{\spsf^2}{N}\left(1 + \frac{\spsf^2}{4d^2}\right)$$

When $d \ll \spsf$ the second term dominates:

$$\sigma_{x_1} \;\approx\; \frac{\spsf^2}{2d\,\sqrt{N}} \tag{3c}$$

Start from Eq. (2b): $\sigma_{x_1}^2 = \sxbar^2 + \frac{1}{4}\sd^2$.

We know $\sxbar \approx \spsf/\sqrt{N}$ (centroid of the combined PSF, same as single emitter). And from Eq. (3b), $\sd \approx \spsf^2/(d\sqrt{N})$. Substituting:

$$\sigma_{x_1}^2 \approx \frac{\spsf^2}{N} + \frac{1}{4}\cdot\frac{\spsf^4}{d^2 N}$$

Factor out $\spsf^2/N$:

$$\sigma_{x_1}^2 \approx \frac{\spsf^2}{N}\left[1 + \frac{\spsf^2}{4d^2}\right]$$

When $d \ll \spsf$ (specifically $d \ll \spsf/2$), the second term $\spsf^2/(4d^2) \gg 1$ dominates, so we drop the first term:

$$\sigma_{x_1}^2 \approx \frac{\spsf^4}{4d^2 N}$$

Taking the square root gives Eq. (3c). The factor of $\spsf/(2d)$ is how much worse you are compared to a single isolated emitter — for $d = 50$ nm and $\spsf = 213$ nm (confocal, 500 nm FWHM) this factor is $213/100 \approx 2.1$, meaning you need $2.1^2 \approx 4.4\times$ more photons just to match the isolated-emitter performance.

Worked example: scan time for $d = 50$ nm, $\sigma_x^\mathrm{target} = 5$ nm

With $\spsf = 213$ nm (confocal, 500 nm FWHM), $d = 50$ nm, and $\sigma_{\mathrm{target}} = 5$ nm, inverting Eq. (3c) gives the required photon count:

$$N \;\sim\; \left(\frac{\spsf^2}{2\,\sigma_{\mathrm{target}}\,d}\right)^{\!2} = \left(\frac{213^2}{2\times 5\times 50}\right)^2 \approx 8{,}200 \text{ photons}$$

To convert to scan time, a confocal raster with pixel size $\Delta x = \spsf/2 \approx 106$ nm and a $\pm 2\spsf$ fit window covers $8\times8 = 64$ pixels. The PSF spreads photons across $\sim 8\pi \approx 25$ effective pixels, so only a fraction $1/(8\pi)$ of the PL rate is collected at any given pixel. Dwell time per pixel and total scan time are:

$$\tau \;=\; \frac{N}{8\pi\cdot\mathrm{PL}} \;\approx\; \frac{8{,}200}{25\times 50{,}000} \;\approx\; 6.6\;\mathrm{ms/pixel}$$ $$T_\mathrm{scan} \;=\; 64\,\tau \;\approx\; 0.42\;\mathrm{s}$$
The Rayleigh prefactor $\spsf/(2d) \approx 2.1$ costs $2.1^2 \approx 4.4\times$ more photons — and therefore $4.4\times$ more scan time — compared to an isolated single NV at the same $\sigma_x$ target. The 0.42 s total is experimentally comfortable. The more serious constraint is that $d$ must be large enough for the result to be self-consistent: the requirement $N > 4\,(\spsf/d)^4$ gives $N > 4\times(213/50)^4 \approx 1{,}300$, which is satisfied here with margin.

Using the $d$-Scaling Before $d$ Is Known

Equations (3b)–(3c) express $\sigma_x$ as a function of $d$, the NV–NV separation. This feels circular: the scaling tells you how accurately you can localize the emitters, but the formula requires knowing $d$ — which is precisely what you are trying to measure. There are three ways to make this productive.

1. The scaling sets the photon budget before the experiment

The relationship $\sigma_x \sim \spsf^2/(2d\,\sqrt{N})$ can be read as a design constraint. Rearranging:

$$N \;\sim\; \left(\frac{\spsf^2}{2\,\sigma_{\mathrm{target}}\,d}\right)^{\!2} \tag{3d}$$

If you have a prior estimate of $d$ — from the implantation dose, crystal stoichiometry, or a rough confocal image — you can use Eq. (3d) to set the required photon budget before committing to a long acquisition. You are not claiming to know $d$ exactly; you are using it as an order-of-magnitude design input, which is exactly the regime where the $\sim$ notation is honest.

To convert photon budget to scan time, note that in a confocal raster scan with pixel size $\Delta x = \spsf/2$ the PSF footprint covers $\sim 8\pi \approx 25$ effective pixels, so only a fraction $1/(8\pi)$ of the PL rate is collected at any given pixel. The total scan time over the fit window ($8\times 8 = 64$ pixels) is:

$$T_\mathrm{scan} \;\approx\; \frac{64}{8\pi}\cdot\frac{N}{\mathrm{PL}} \;\approx\; \frac{2.5\,N}{\mathrm{PL}} \tag{3e}$$

The factor 2.5 is purely geometric: for a 2D Gaussian of width $\spsf$ discretised on pixels of size $\Delta x = \spsf/2$, the PSF solid angle in pixel units is $2\pi\spsf^2/\Delta x^2 = 8\pi$ (integrating the Gaussian within the $1\sigma$ radius). The factor $64/(8\pi) \approx 2.5$ therefore equals the ratio of fit-window area to PSF area in pixel units and is convention-dependent at the order-unity level: using a $2\sigma$ inclusion radius gives $16\pi \approx 50$ effective pixels and a factor of $64/50 \approx 1.3$ instead. All scan time estimates carry a factor-of-2 geometric uncertainty that does not affect the exponential scalings.

Confocal resolution 500 nm (FWHM) gives $\spsf = 500/2.35 \approx 213$ nm. With $d = 50$ nm and $\sigma_{\mathrm{target}} = 5$ nm (10% of $d$, a practical target), Eq. (3d) gives:

$$N \sim \left(\frac{213^2}{2\times 5\times 50}\right)^2 = \left(\frac{45{,}369}{500}\right)^2 \approx 91^2 \approx 8{,}200 \text{ photons}$$

The Rayleigh prefactor $\spsf/(2d) = 213/100 \approx 2.1$ means you need $2.1^2 \approx 4.4\times$ more photons than for an isolated emitter at the same accuracy.

Converting to scan time

In a confocal raster scan, photons arrive at a rate set by the PSF overlap between beam and emitter. With pixel size $\Delta x = \spsf/2 \approx 106$ nm (2 pixels per $\spsf$, sufficient for Nyquist sampling) and a fit window of $\pm 2\spsf$, the scan grid is $8\times 8 = 64$ pixels. The total photons collected across all pixels equals the per-pixel dwell time $\tau$ times the PL rate summed over the PSF footprint:

$$N = \tau \cdot \mathrm{PL} \times \sum_\mathrm{pixels} e^{-|\mathbf{r}_i|^2/2\spsf^2} \approx \tau \cdot \mathrm{PL} \cdot \frac{2\pi\spsf^2}{\Delta x^2} = \tau \cdot \mathrm{PL} \cdot 8\pi$$

Solving for the dwell time per pixel:

$$\tau = \frac{N}{8\pi \cdot \mathrm{PL}} \approx \frac{8{,}200}{25 \times 50{,}000} \approx 6.6\;\mathrm{ms/pixel}$$

The total scan time over all 64 pixels is:

$$T_\mathrm{scan} = 64\,\tau \approx 64 \times 6.6\;\mathrm{ms} \approx 0.42\;\mathrm{s}$$

This is a factor of $64/(8\pi) \approx 2.5$ longer than the naive estimate $N/\mathrm{PL} = 0.16$ s, which assumed all photons arrive at a single pixel. The factor 2.5 is purely geometric and does not depend on $d$ or $\spsf$.

For comparison, shrinking to a superresolution PSF of 50 nm FWHM ($\spsf^\mathrm{eff} \approx 21$ nm) puts $d = 50\,\mathrm{nm} \gg \spsf^\mathrm{eff}$. The Rayleigh curse vanishes and $N \sim (21/5)^2 \approx 18$ photons suffice. But the pixel size shrinks to $\spsf^\mathrm{eff}/2 \approx 11$ nm, so a comparable field of view requires $(213/21)^2 \approx 100\times$ more pixels — the photon budget per pixel drops dramatically even if total scan time stays similar.

2. Self-consistency: the estimate of $d$ can come from the same dataset

In practice you do not need $d$ as a fixed external input. A maximum-likelihood fit to the same $N$-photon image returns a joint estimate $(\hat x_1, \hat x_2)$, from which $\hat d = |\hat x_2 - \hat x_1|$. The uncertainty on this estimate is precisely $\sd$ from Eq. (3b). The standard CRB analysis is self-consistent: $d$ in the bound refers to the true (unknown) $d$, and $\hat d$ converges to it as $N\to\infty$ at the rate the bound predicts.

A useful check: evaluate $\sd/\hat d$ after the fit. If this ratio is large (say $> 0.5$), the estimate of $d$ is unreliable, and the reported $\sigma_x$ should be treated with caution regardless of the raw photon count. This ratio is itself an observable diagnostic.

Self-consistency rule of thumb: require $\hat d > 2\,\sd$ before trusting a localization result. Substituting $\sd \approx \spsf^2/(\hat d\sqrt{N})$ this becomes $\hat d^2 > 2\,\spsf^2/\sqrt{N}$, or equivalently $N > 4\,(\spsf/\hat d)^4$. For $d = 50$ nm and $\spsf = 213$ nm (confocal, 500 nm FWHM) this requires $N > 4\times(213/50)^4 \approx 1{,}300$ photons — satisfied at 50 kcps in under 30 ms, so the $d=50$ nm case is self-consistent on confocal. Contrast with $d = 10$ nm: the threshold rises to $N > 4\times(213/10)^4 \approx 8\times 10^7$, or 27 minutes at 50 kcps — firmly in the self-inconsistent regime.

3. The scaling is a bound on what any estimator can achieve

The most important reinterpretation: the CRB does not depend on which estimator you use. It is a lower bound on $\sigma_x$ for every unbiased estimator, regardless of algorithm. If someone reports $\sigma_x = 0.5$ nm for a 5 nm pair with $N = 10^4$ photons, the bound says that is impossible unless the estimator is biased. You do not need to know $d$ to use the CRB this way — you plug in the claimed $d$ and check whether the reported $\sigma_x$ is even physically consistent.

In this sense the formula with $d$ in it is not circular but adversarial: the better the claimed localization, the tighter the constraint you can place on the minimum photon count required to achieve it.

$M$ NV Centers with Known $M$: the Combinatorial Catastrophe

Density penalty

For $M$ emitters uniformly distributed in a disk of radius $\spsf$, the mean nearest-neighbor separation is:

$$\bar d \;\sim\; \frac{\spsf}{\sqrt{M}} \tag{4a}$$

We have $M$ points distributed uniformly in a disk of area $A = \pi\spsf^2$, giving number density $\rho = M/A = M/(\pi\spsf^2)$.

For a uniform 2D Poisson point process with density $\rho$, the probability that the nearest neighbor is further than distance $r$ is the probability of finding zero other points within a disk of radius $r$:

$$P(\text{nearest neighbor} > r) = e^{-\rho \pi r^2}$$

This is just the Poisson zero-count probability. The mean nearest-neighbor distance is:

$$\E[r_{\min}] = \int_0^\infty P(r_{\min} > r)\,dr = \int_0^\infty e^{-\rho\pi r^2}\,dr = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{\rho}}$$

The integral is a Gaussian: $\int_0^\infty e^{-\alpha r^2}dr = \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{\pi/\alpha}$, so with $\alpha = \rho\pi$ we get $\frac{1}{2}\sqrt{1/\rho}$.

Substituting $\rho = M/(\pi\spsf^2)$:

$$\bar d = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{M/(\pi\spsf^2)}} = \frac{\spsf}{2}\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{M}} \sim \frac{\spsf}{\sqrt{M}}$$

absorbing the numerical prefactor $\sqrt{\pi}/2 \approx 0.89$ into the $\sim$ sign, since we only care about the scaling.

Substituting $\bar d \sim \spsf/\sqrt{M}$ into Eq. (3c):

$$\sx \;\sim\; \frac{\spsf\,\sqrt{M}}{\sqrt{N}} \tag{4b}$$

From Eq. (3c), the individual position uncertainty for two nearby emitters separated by $d$ is $\sigma \approx \spsf^2/(2d\sqrt{N})$. Replace $d$ with the typical nearest-neighbor separation $\bar d \sim \spsf/\sqrt{M}$:

$$\sx \sim \frac{\spsf^2}{2\,(\spsf/\sqrt{M})\,\sqrt{N}} = \frac{\spsf^2 \sqrt{M}}{2\,\spsf\,\sqrt{N}} = \frac{\spsf\sqrt{M}}{2\sqrt{N}}$$

Dropping the factor of 2 (order-of-magnitude scaling):

$$\sx \sim \frac{\spsf\sqrt{M}}{\sqrt{N}}$$

The $\sqrt{M}$ growth makes sense: more emitters $\Rightarrow$ smaller $\bar d$ $\Rightarrow$ stronger Rayleigh curse on each pair $\Rightarrow$ each emitter is harder to localize. Note that the $\sqrt{M}$ factor means you need $M$ times more photons to maintain the same $\sigma_x$ as $M$ grows — but since the total photon budget is also shared over $M$ emitters, this is a genuinely bad situation.

Permutation explosion and Fisher matrix ill-conditioning

With $M$ emitters the likelihood surface has $M!$ peaks. The Fisher information matrix becomes ill-conditioned; its smallest eigenvalue scales as:

$$\lambda_{\min} \;\propto\; \left(\frac{\bar d}{\spsf}\right)^{2(M-1)} N \tag{4c}$$

The Fisher information matrix $\mathbf{F}$ for the $2M$ position parameters has entries:

$$F_{ij} = N\int \frac{(\partial p/\partial\theta_i)(\partial p/\partial\theta_j)}{p}\,dx$$

where $\theta_i$ are the $2M$ position coordinates. Consider the eigenvector corresponding to the smallest eigenvalue: it is the mode of parameter variation to which the image is least sensitive. For $M$ emitters this is a collective rearrangement that moves all emitters while barely changing the total intensity profile.

Physically, the worst case is the "accordion mode" where emitters move simultaneously in a pattern that is nearly invisible at the detector. Each pairwise Rayleigh curse contributes a factor of $(d/\spsf)^2$ to the determinant of the Fisher matrix. With $M-1$ independent separations (one centroid + $M-1$ relative positions), the determinant scales as:

$$\det(\mathbf{F}) \propto N^{2M} \left(\frac{\bar d}{\spsf}\right)^{2(M-1)}$$

The smallest eigenvalue is roughly the determinant divided by the product of all other eigenvalues. The centroid eigenvalue stays $\sim N/\spsf^2$ regardless of $d$. After dividing out the $M-1$ "easy" eigenvalues (which also scale as $N/\spsf^2$), the remaining smallest one scales as:

$$\lambda_{\min} \propto N\left(\frac{\bar d}{\spsf}\right)^{2(M-1)}$$

This goes to zero exponentially fast in $M$ (since $\bar d/\spsf = 1/\sqrt{M} < 1$), explaining why the problem becomes rapidly intractable as $M$ grows.

The corresponding worst-case position uncertainty — obtained by taking $\sigma_{\max}^2 \geq 1/\lambda_{\min}$ and substituting Eq. (4c) — is:

$$\sigma_{\max} \;\sim\; \frac{\spsf^M}{\bar d^{\,M-1}\,\sqrt{N}} \tag{4d}$$

Note: Eq. (4d) is not an independent result — it is the same as the Rayleigh-compounded part of Eq. (5b) below, without the model-selection and density factors. It is retained here only for pedagogical continuity; Eq. (5b) is the complete expression and should be used for numerical estimates.

The CRB for the worst-case direction in parameter space is $\sigma_{\max}^2 \geq 1/\lambda_{\min}$. Using Eq. (4c) with $\bar d = \spsf/\sqrt{M}$:

$$\sigma_{\max}^2 \sim \frac{1}{N} \cdot \left(\frac{\spsf}{\bar d}\right)^{2(M-1)} = \frac{\spsf^{2(M-1)}}{\bar d^{\,2(M-1)}\,N}$$

Taking the square root:

$$\sigma_{\max} \sim \frac{\spsf^{M-1}}{\bar d^{\,M-1}\,\sqrt{N}} = \frac{\spsf^{M-1}\,\spsf}{\bar d^{\,M-1}\,\sqrt{N}} = \frac{\spsf^{M}}{\bar d^{\,M-1}\,\sqrt{N}}$$

This is exponential in $M$: substituting $\bar d = \spsf/\sqrt{M}$ gives $\sigma_{\max} \sim \spsf\,M^{(M-1)/2}/\sqrt{N}$, which grows faster than any polynomial in $M$.

Worked example: scan time for $M^* = 5$, $d = 50$ nm, known $M$

Using Eq. (4b) with $\spsf = 213$ nm, $M^* = 5$, and $\sigma_{\mathrm{target}} = 5$ nm:

$$N \;\sim\; \left(\frac{\spsf\sqrt{M^*}}{\sigma_{\mathrm{target}}}\right)^{\!2} = \left(\frac{213\times\sqrt{5}}{5}\right)^{\!2} \approx (95)^2 \approx 9{,}100 \text{ photons}$$

Applying Eq. (3e) with PL = 50 kcps:

$$T_\mathrm{scan} \;\approx\; \frac{2.5\times 9{,}100}{50{,}000} \;\approx\; 0.45\;\mathrm{s}$$
This is only marginally worse than the 2-NV case (0.41 s) because the $\sqrt{M^*} = \sqrt{5} \approx 2.2$ density penalty costs $M^* = 5\times$ more photons but the log scale is forgiving. The much larger cost is hidden in the assumption that $M$ is known — the unknown-$M$ case (§6) costs orders of magnitude more.

The General Case: $M$ Unknown

The most honest version of the problem: $M$ is unknown. We must simultaneously select the model (how many emitters?) and estimate parameters.

Three regimes of $N$

Low-$N$ regime. Model selection error dominates — wrongly identifying $M$ introduces a bias that does not shrink as $1/\sqrt{N}$.

Intermediate regime. The correct $M^*$ is identified with high probability above a threshold:

$$N_{\mathrm{threshold}} \;\sim\; \left(\frac{\spsf}{d_{\min}}\right)^{2M^*} \tag{5a}$$

To correctly identify $M^*$ vs $M^*-1$ emitters, the likelihood ratio between the two models must be statistically distinguishable from noise. The log-likelihood difference between the true model ($M^*$ emitters) and the simpler model ($M^*-1$ emitters) grows as the Fisher information for the "extra" separation that the simpler model misses.

From Eq. (3a), the Fisher information for one separation at distance $d$ is $\propto d^2/\spsf^2$. The likelihood ratio test statistic for detecting one extra emitter at separation $d$ from the existing cluster scales as $N \cdot (d/\spsf)^2$. We can correctly detect the extra emitter when this exceeds a threshold of order 1:

$$N \cdot \frac{d_{\min}^2}{\spsf^2} \gtrsim 1 \quad\Rightarrow\quad N \gtrsim \frac{\spsf^2}{d_{\min}^2}$$

But this argument must be applied $M^*-1$ times — once for each successive emitter being added to the model. Each additional emitter contributes another factor of $(\spsf/d_{\min})^2$ to the threshold (because each new separation has its own Rayleigh curse). Multiplying $M^*-1$ factors together:

$$N_{\mathrm{threshold}} \sim \left(\frac{\spsf}{d_{\min}}\right)^{2(M^*-1)} \sim \left(\frac{\spsf}{d_{\min}}\right)^{2M^*}$$

absorbing the $-1$ in the exponent into the $\sim$. This exponential growth in $M^*$ is why model selection becomes astronomically hard for densely packed emitters: for $d_{\min} = \spsf/10$ and $M^* = 5$ you need $10^{10}$ photons just to correctly count the emitters, before you even start localizing them.

Which model comparison does this correspond to? The exponent $2M^*$ arises from the full sequence of $M^*-1$ incremental tests (each costing a factor $(\spsf/d_\mathrm{min})^2$), equivalent to testing $M = M^*$ vs $M = 0$ at the cluster patch. The more conservative test — distinguishing $M^*$ from $M^*-1$ in isolation — gives exponent $2(M^*-1)$, which for $M^*=5$ differs by a factor of $(\spsf/d_\mathrm{min})^2 \approx 18$ in $N_\mathrm{threshold}$. Both scale exponentially in $M^*$ and the practical conclusion is unchanged; the precise value depends on the test statistic and prior.

High-$N$ regime. Once $N \gg N_{\mathrm{threshold}}$, the correct $M^*$ is selected reliably and the model-selection penalty $g \to 1$. The remaining error is governed purely by the Rayleigh curse on the resolved separations:

$$\sx \;\sim\; \frac{\spsf^{M^*}}{d_{\min}^{M^*-1}\,\sqrt{N}} \tag{5a$'$}$$

This is the same as the known-$M$ worst case from Eq. (4d). The important message is that successfully identifying $M^*$ does not rescue you from the Rayleigh curse — it merely removes the additional systematic bias due to model mismatch. For densely packed emitters with $d_{\min} \ll \spsf$, this regime may only be reached at photon counts that are physically unrealizable, making the intermediate regime effectively the permanent operating condition.

Practical implication: With $d_{\min} = 50$ nm, $M^* = 5$, and confocal $\spsf \approx 213$ nm, the ratio $\spsf/d_{\min} \approx 4.3$. The model-selection threshold is $N_{\mathrm{threshold}} \sim (4.3)^{10} \approx 2\times 10^6$ photons — about 98 s at 50 kcps using the raster scan formula Eq. (3e). But once past the threshold, the Rayleigh factor $(\spsf/d_{\min})^{M^*-1} = (4.3)^4 \approx 340$ multiplies $\sigma_x$, meaning you need $340^2 \approx 10^5$ times more photons than the single-emitter bound just to match isolated-emitter accuracy — completely out of reach. With superresolution ($\spsf^{\mathrm{eff}} \approx 21$ nm, 50 nm FWHM), $\spsf^{\mathrm{eff}}/d_{\min} \approx 0.43 < 1$: $M_{\mathrm{eff}} \leq 1$ and all multi-emitter pathologies vanish. This is the quantitative argument for why superresolution (§7) is not a luxury but a necessity once $d_{\min}$ falls below $\spsf$.

Combined scaling

This is the central result of the note. Every factor has a distinct physical origin and can be attacked independently:

$$\sx \;\sim\; \underbrace{\frac{\spsf}{\sqrt{N}}}_{\displaystyle\text{single-emitter}} \times \underbrace{\sqrt{M^*}}_{\displaystyle\text{density}} \times \underbrace{\!\left(\frac{\spsf}{d_{\min}}\right)^{\!M^*-1}}_{\displaystyle\text{Rayleigh (compounded)}} \times \underbrace{g(N,M^*,d_{\min})}_{\displaystyle\text{model selection}} \tag{5b}$$

The four factors each come from a different layer of the problem:

Factor 1: $\spsf/\sqrt{N}$

This is the single-emitter CRB from Eq. (1). It is always present as a floor — you cannot do better than this even in ideal conditions.

Factor 2: $\sqrt{M^*}$

From Eq. (4b). Packing $M^*$ emitters into a diffraction volume reduces the typical nearest-neighbor separation to $\bar d \sim \spsf/\sqrt{M^*}$, which degrades each emitter's localization by $\sqrt{M^*}$ via the Rayleigh curse.

Factor 3: $(\spsf/d_{\min})^{M^*-1}$

From Eq. (4c)–(4d). The Fisher matrix ill-conditioning compounds over all $M^*-1$ independent separations, each contributing a factor of $\spsf/d_{\min}$. This is the dominant term when $M^*$ is large and $d_{\min} \ll \spsf$.

Factor 4: $g(N, M^*, d_{\min})$

The model-selection penalty. When $N < N_{\mathrm{threshold}}$ from Eq. (5a), $g \gg 1$ because you are likely fitting the wrong $M$ and the positional errors have a large systematic component. Once $N \gg N_{\mathrm{threshold}}$, $g \to 1$ and only the first three factors matter. In practice for $M^* \geq 4$ at sub-diffraction densities, $g$ never reaches 1 with realistic photon budgets.

When prior information rescues the counting problem

The threshold $N_\mathrm{threshold} \sim (\spsf/d_\mathrm{min})^{2M^*}$ assumes uniform priors on $M$ and on emitter positions — the worst case. When structural prior information is available, the effective threshold drops dramatically.

For NV centers in a lattice (known implantation geometry, crystal sites, or a diamond vacancy lattice), the Bayesian model evidence integrates out over position priors constrained to lattice sites with width $\sigma_\mathrm{site} \ll a_L$:

$$P(\mathrm{data}\mid M) = \int d^{2M}\mathbf{r}\ P(\mathrm{data}\mid\mathbf{r})\,\pi(\mathbf{r}\mid\mathrm{lattice})\,\pi(M\mid\bar n)$$

With a Poisson occupancy prior (mean $\bar n \lesssim 1$ per site), brightness alone — total PL in the cluster, which steps by $N_1$ per occupied site with Poisson noise $\sqrt{M N_1}$ — can discriminate $M$ when $N_1 \gtrsim M$, i.e. a few hundred photons per NV. This shifts the bottleneck from model counting back to localization (§5), which is then handled by superresolution (§7).

Practical implication for lattice experiments: if you know the lattice constant $a_L$ and have a prior on $\bar n$ from implantation dose, the frequentist $N_\mathrm{threshold}$ is pessimistic by orders of magnitude. Bayesian evidence with a tight positional prior can correctly identify $M^*$ from brightness alone at $\sim 10^2$–$10^3$ photons per emitter — well within a single confocal scan. The harder problem then shifts to localization accuracy, not emitter counting.

How Superresolution Rescues the Problem

All pathologies are controlled by one quantity: the number of emitters per resolution element,

$$M_{\mathrm{eff}} \;=\; \rho\,\bigl(\seff\bigr)^2 \tag{6a}$$

$\rho$ is the 2D number density of NV centers (emitters per nm²), fixed by sample preparation. $\seff$ is the effective PSF radius of the imaging system, which superresolution can shrink below the diffraction limit. The product $\rho \cdot (\seff)^2$ is a dimensionless number: it counts how many emitters fall within an area of $\seff^2$ — i.e., within one resolution element.

More precisely: the area of a disk of radius $\seff$ is $\pi(\seff)^2$; the expected number of emitters within it is $\rho\pi(\seff)^2$. The formula absorbs the $\pi$ into the $\sim$ notation, since we only care about the scaling.

Why does this control everything? Look back at the scaling results:

  • Eq. (4b): $\sx \sim \spsf\sqrt{M}/\sqrt{N}$ — depends on $M$ within the PSF area, i.e. $M_\mathrm{eff}$.
  • Eq. (4d): $\sigma_\mathrm{max} \sim \spsf^M/(\bar d^{M-1}\sqrt{N})$ — depends on $M_\mathrm{eff}$ through $M$ and $\bar d \sim \spsf/\sqrt{M_\mathrm{eff}}$.
  • Eq. (5a): $N_\mathrm{threshold} \sim (\spsf/d_\mathrm{min})^{2M^*}$ — depends on how many emitters are packed within $\spsf$.

When $M_\mathrm{eff} \leq 1$: $M=1$ within the resolution element, all multi-emitter pathologies vanish ($1! = 1$, Fisher matrix is $1\times 1$ and trivially well-conditioned), and we recover $\sx = \seff/\sqrt{N}$.

Deterministic PSF narrowing (STED, GSD, spin-RESOLFT). Shrink $\seff$ by factor $f$: this reduces $M_\mathrm{eff}$ by $f^2$. The cost is scan time $\propto f^2$ (more pixels for the same field of view).

$$\seff \to \frac{\seff}{f} \;\Rightarrow\; M_\mathrm{eff} \to \frac{M_\mathrm{eff}}{f^2} \tag{6b}$$

From Eq. (6a): $M_\mathrm{eff} = \rho\,(\seff)^2$. If we reduce $\seff$ by a factor $f$ (i.e. $\seff \to \seff/f$), then:

$$M_\mathrm{eff}^{\mathrm{new}} = \rho\,\left(\frac{\seff}{f}\right)^2 = \frac{\rho\,\seff^2}{f^2} = \frac{M_\mathrm{eff}}{f^2}$$

The quadratic dependence on PSF width is because the resolution element is 2D — its area shrinks as the square of the linear dimension. This is why a factor of 10 improvement in resolution (say STED achieving 25 nm instead of 250 nm) gives a factor of 100 reduction in $M_\mathrm{eff}$, which can easily take a sample from $M_\mathrm{eff} \gg 1$ (intractable) to $M_\mathrm{eff} \ll 1$ (trivially solvable).

Stochastic emitter thinning (STORM, single-spin STORM). Keep $\seff \approx 250$ nm but reduce $\rho_\mathrm{eff}$ per frame by switching most emitters off. Each frame has $M_\mathrm{eff} \ll 1$; the full image is reconstructed over many frames at a time cost of $1/(\text{active fraction})$.

Targeted excitation (MINFLUX). A third strategy: rather than imaging the full FOV, place an excitation doughnut's zero at sequential positions near a suspected emitter and read photon counts as a function of beam position. The CRB scales as $\sigma_x = L/\sqrt{8N}$ where $L$ is the orbit radius (Balzarotti et al. Science 2017). With $L \ll \spsf$, MINFLUX is more photon-efficient than confocal by a factor $(\spsf/L)^2$ at the same precision — sub-nm localization with $\sim 10^2$ photons is achievable. For NV centers, photostability makes MINFLUX particularly attractive; the main requirement is isolating one emitter per targeted acquisition (combine with stochastic thinning or spin-contrast for clusters).

In both cases superresolution does not improve the $1/\sqrt{N}$ scaling — it rescues the prefactor by enforcing $M_\mathrm{eff} \leq 1$.

Worked example: scan time with superresolution, $d = 50$ nm

With $\seff = 21.3$ nm (50 nm FWHM) and $d = 50\,\mathrm{nm} \gg \seff$, the pair is well-resolved and Eq. (1) applies:

$$N \;\sim\; \left(\frac{\seff}{\sigma_{\mathrm{target}}}\right)^{\!2} = \left(\frac{21.3}{5}\right)^{\!2} \approx 18 \text{ photons}$$

Applying Eq. (3e) with PL = 50 kcps gives $T_\mathrm{scan} \approx 2.5\times18/50{,}000 \approx 0.9$ ms. However, the pixel size shrinks to $\seff/2 \approx 11$ nm. Scanning the same physical field of view as confocal requires $(213/21.3)^2 = 100\times$ more pixels, so the total imaging time for a fixed area scales as:

$$T_\mathrm{FOV} \;=\; T_\mathrm{scan} \times \left(\frac{\spsf}{\seff}\right)^{\!2} \;\approx\; 0.9\;\mathrm{ms} \times 100 \;=\; 0.09\;\mathrm{s}$$
Per-emitter localization is 460× faster with superresolution (0.9 ms vs. 0.41 s) because the Rayleigh curse vanishes. The $100\times$ pixel overhead for the same field of view partially offsets this, but the net gain is still enormous — and more importantly, superresolution is the only route that avoids the exponential scaling catastrophe of the unknown-$M$ case.

Effect of Unequal Emitter Intensities

All preceding sections assumed equal emitter intensities. When $I_1 \neq I_2$ (or more generally $I_i \neq I_j$), the permutation symmetry that drove the Rayleigh curse is broken, and the Fisher information gains a floor that is independent of $d$.

Two-emitter case: symmetry breaking at $d = 0$

The Rayleigh curse arose because the combined PSF of two equal emitters is symmetric under exchange — at $d = 0$ they are indistinguishable, forcing $\partial p/\partial d = 0$ and therefore $I(d) \propto d^2$. With unequal intensities the combined profile is:

$$p(x \mid d) = \frac{I_1}{I_\mathrm{tot}}\,G(x - d/2) + \frac{I_2}{I_\mathrm{tot}}\,G(x + d/2)$$

where $I_\mathrm{tot} = I_1 + I_2$. Now $\partial p/\partial d \neq 0$ at $d = 0$ because swapping the two emitters moves the brighter one to where the dimmer one was — a detectable first-order change. Define the asymmetry parameter:

$$\alpha \;=\; \frac{I_1 - I_2}{I_1 + I_2} \;\in\; [0,\,1] \tag{7a}$$

The Fisher information for $d$ becomes:

$$I(d) \;\propto\; \frac{\alpha^2 + (d/\spsf)^2}{\spsf^2} \tag{7b}$$

With unequal weights, the combined profile is $p = w_1 G(x-d/2) + w_2 G(x+d/2)$ where $w_k = I_k/I_\mathrm{tot}$. Differentiating with respect to $d$:

$$\frac{\partial p}{\partial d} = \frac{1}{2}\left[-w_1 G'(x-d/2) + w_2 G'(x+d/2)\right]$$

At $d = 0$, $G'(x-d/2) = G'(x+d/2) = G'(x)$, so:

$$\left.\frac{\partial p}{\partial d}\right|_{d=0} = \frac{w_2 - w_1}{2}\,G'(x) = -\frac{\alpha}{2}\,G'(x)$$

This is non-zero when $\alpha \neq 0$. The Fisher information at $d = 0$ is therefore:

$$I(0) = \int \frac{[\alpha G'(x)/2]^2}{G(x)}\,dx = \frac{\alpha^2}{4}\int \frac{[G'(x)]^2}{G(x)}\,dx = \frac{\alpha^2}{4}\cdot\frac{1}{\spsf^2}$$

The integral $\int [G'(x)]^2/G(x)\,dx = 1/\spsf^2$ is the Fisher information for the mean of a Gaussian. For finite $d$, the Taylor expansion adds the $d^2/\spsf^4$ term from Eq. (3a), giving the combined result $I(d) \propto (\alpha^2 + d^2/\spsf^2)/\spsf^2$.

The CRB on separation and individual positions now become:

$$\sd \;\gtrsim\; \frac{\spsf}{\sqrt{N}\,\sqrt{\alpha^2 + (d/\spsf)^2}} \tag{7c}$$
$$\sigma_{x_1} \;\sim\; \frac{\spsf}{\sqrt{N}\,\sqrt{\alpha^2 + (d/\spsf)^2}} \tag{7d}$$

Two limiting cases and the crossover

When $d \ll \alpha\,\spsf$ (asymmetry dominates):

$$\sigma_{x_1} \;\sim\; \frac{\spsf}{\alpha\sqrt{N}}$$

The curse is gone — $\sigma_{x_1}$ is finite even at $d = 0$, recovering essentially single-emitter performance scaled by $1/\alpha$.

When $d \gg \alpha\,\spsf$ (separation dominates):

$$\sigma_{x_1} \;\sim\; \frac{\spsf^2}{d\sqrt{N}}$$

The original Rayleigh curse — asymmetry is irrelevant once $d$ is large enough.

The crossover occurs at $d^* \sim \alpha\,\spsf$. For your confocal setup ($\spsf = 213$ nm, $d = 50$ nm), asymmetry helps only when $\alpha \gtrsim d/\spsf \approx 0.23$, i.e. a 62%/38% or more unequal intensity split.

$M$-emitter generalization

For $M$ emitters with intensities $\{I_1,\ldots,I_M\}$, the relevant quantity is the pairwise asymmetry of the most similar pair — the hardest pair for the fit to distinguish:

$$\alpha_{\min} \;=\; \min_{i \neq j} \frac{|I_i - I_j|}{I_\mathrm{tot}} \tag{7e}$$

The smallest Fisher matrix eigenvalue generalizes to:

$$\lambda_{\min} \;\propto\; N \cdot \left[\alpha_{\min}^2 + \left(\frac{d_{\min}}{\spsf}\right)^{\!2}\right]^{M-1} \tag{7f}$$

and the worst-case position uncertainty becomes:

$$\sigma_{\max} \;\sim\; \frac{\spsf}{\sqrt{N}\,\left[\alpha_{\min}^2 + (d_{\min}/\spsf)^2\right]^{(M-1)/2}} \tag{7g}$$

Note on derivation status: Eq. (7g) is a heuristic interpolation between the rigorous $M=2$ result (Eqs. 7b–d, traceable to Chao, Ward & Ober 2013) and the equal-intensity compounding (Eq. 4c). A rigorous derivation for $M > 2$ would require evaluating the full $M$-emitter Fisher matrix with non-degenerate intensities, which is not done here. The qualitative claim — that intensity asymmetry sets a floor on $\lambda_\mathrm{min}$ independent of $d$, and that this floor compounds as $(M-1)$ — is robust; the exact functional form of the interpolation is not.

Setting $\alpha_\mathrm{min} = 0$ in Eq. (7g):

$$\sigma_\mathrm{max} \sim \frac{\spsf}{\sqrt{N}\,(d_\mathrm{min}/\spsf)^{M-1}} = \frac{\spsf^M}{d_\mathrm{min}^{M-1}\sqrt{N}}$$

which is exactly Eq. (4d). Setting $d_\mathrm{min} = 0$:

$$\sigma_\mathrm{max} \sim \frac{\spsf}{\alpha_\mathrm{min}^{M-1}\sqrt{N}}$$

The compounding is now in $\alpha_\mathrm{min}$ rather than $d_\mathrm{min}/\spsf$ — still exponential in $M$ but no longer diverges as $d\to 0$.

How to engineer asymmetry with NV centers

Identical NVs in identical environments have $\alpha \lesssim 0.05$ — intrinsic intensity asymmetry is negligible. Two practical routes can increase it:

1. Crystallographic orientation. NV$^-$ centers have four possible C$_3$ axes along $\langle 111 \rangle$ directions. NVs along different axes have different transition-dipole orientations relative to the excitation polarization. With linearly polarized excitation, the PL contrast between cross-polarized NVs can reach $\alpha \sim 0.5$; adding a polarization analyzer in detection pushes this to $\alpha \sim 0.8$. The four-orientation random distribution means that in an unaligned ensemble roughly half of all NV pairs include cross-polarized emitters, providing asymmetry automatically with no extra hardware beyond a polarizer.

2. Strain-shifted ZPL at low temperature. Below $\sim 20$ K each NV$^-$ has a sharp zero-phonon line (ZPL) at 637 nm whose center shifts with local strain by typically MHz–GHz. A spectral filter or tunable narrowband excitation can selectively address individual NVs — effectively $\alpha = 1$ per spectral channel. This is the deterministic equivalent of STORM spectral demultiplexing, but without blinking. Requires cryogenic operation.

Polarization is the low-cost option ($\alpha \sim 0.5$ from random orientations, no cryogenics); spectral ZPL demultiplexing is the high-performance option ($\alpha \to 1$, but requires $T \lesssim 20$ K). From Table 2, the difference between $\alpha_\mathrm{min} = 0.5$ and $\alpha_\mathrm{min} = 0$ is a $\sim 100\times$ reduction in required integration time for $M^*=5$ — a meaningful gain for room-temperature experiments where cryogenics is impractical.

Why Confocal Alone Cannot Resolve Five NV Centers: Practical Barriers

The preceding sections established the information-theoretic limits — what is achievable with the best possible algorithm given a perfect image. This section explains why, in practice, nobody uses confocal imaging alone to resolve five NV centers in a cluster, even for modest 50 nm accuracy. There are four distinct barriers, each independently sufficient to make the experiment impractical, and they compound.

Barrier 1: the image looks like a single NV

A cluster of five NV centers with $d_\mathrm{min} = 50$ nm occupies a region of $\sim 100$ nm across. The confocal PSF has $\spsf = 213$ nm — the cluster is more than $4\times$ smaller than the resolution element. The recorded image is an unresolved blob that is visually indistinguishable from a single bright NV. There is no obvious signature in the image — not a broadened profile, not a double peak, not an asymmetry — that would alert the experimenter that five emitters are present rather than one.

Before any localization can begin, the correct model ($M^* = 5$) must be identified. From Eq. (5a), the photon threshold for correctly distinguishing $M^*$ from $M^*-1$ emitters is:

$$N_\mathrm{threshold} \sim \left(\frac{\spsf}{d_\mathrm{min}}\right)^{2M^*} = (4.3)^{10} \approx 2\times 10^6 \text{ photons} \quad(\approx 98\;\mathrm{s\;using\;Eq.\;(3e)})$$

This 98 s is the cost just to correctly count the emitters — before any attempt to localize them. And this is the theoretical minimum; real model selection tests (BIC, likelihood ratio, AIC) require considerably more photons to achieve reliable decisions in practice.

The 50 nm accuracy case does not escape this. Requiring only 50 nm accuracy instead of 5 nm reduces the localization photon budget from $2\times10^8$ to $\sim 2\times10^4$ — a factor of $10^4$ cheaper. But $N_\mathrm{threshold} \approx 2\times10^6$ is independent of the accuracy target. You must still solve the counting problem first, and that costs the same regardless of how accurately you want to localize afterward.

Barrier 2: the optimization is computationally intractable

Suppose you have collected enough photons and you correctly know $M^* = 5$. The MLE requires maximizing the likelihood over 10 position parameters $(x_1, y_1, \ldots, x_5, y_5)$. The likelihood surface has $M^*! = 5! = 120$ local maxima corresponding to all permutations of emitter labels. Each maximum is surrounded by a narrow basin — narrow because the PSFs overlap strongly and small position changes produce nearly identical images.

Finding the global maximum of a non-convex function with 120 local maxima in 10 dimensions requires either:

a) An exhaustive search with good initialization for all 120 basins — but you need a starting point within each basin, and with an unresolved image there is no obvious way to generate those initializations.

b) A stochastic global optimizer (simulated annealing, basin hopping) — these work but their runtime scales poorly with $M^*$ and the convergence criterion is hard to verify when the likelihood surface is nearly flat in the $d$-direction.

In the sub-diffraction regime the Fisher matrix condition number $\kappa \sim (\spsf/d_\mathrm{min})^{2(M^*-1)} \approx (4.3)^8 \approx 10^5$ means the likelihood surface is $10^5$ times more curved in the easy centroid direction than in the hard separation direction. Standard gradient-based optimizers follow the curvature and immediately collapse to the centroid basin, never exploring the separation directions. This is not a failure of the algorithm — it is the geometry of the problem.

Barrier 3: the gap between the CRB and any real estimator

The CRB is achieved only by the maximum-likelihood estimator, and only asymptotically — meaning in the limit $N \to \infty$ with the true model known. At finite $N$, all practical estimators have variance strictly larger than the CRB. For the multi-emitter sub-diffraction problem, the gap is not a small correction:

The CRB derivation assumes the estimator operates near the correct global maximum of the likelihood. In the presence of 120 local maxima, any estimator that is not guaranteed to find the global maximum will sometimes converge to a wrong permutation — introducing a bias of order $d_\mathrm{min}$ that does not shrink with $N$. For $d_\mathrm{min} = 50$ nm and a 50 nm accuracy target, a single permutation error produces an error comparable to the entire accuracy budget. The probability of such an error only falls below 1% once $N \gg N_\mathrm{threshold}$, which from Barrier 1 is already $2\times 10^6$ photons.

In practice the effective variance of real estimators on $M = 5$ sub-diffraction emitters is $10\times$–$100\times$ the CRB variance, meaning the true required integration time is $10\times$–$100\times$ longer than Table 1 suggests.

Barrier 4: the physical model is wrong during a long integration

Even if barriers 1–3 were solved, the experiment itself violates the assumptions of the model over the timescales required:

Charge state switching. NV centers stochastically convert between NV$^-$ (useful signal) and NV$^0$ (no useful signal) under laser illumination on timescales of seconds to minutes. During the $\sim$98 s model-selection integration, the effective number of emitters $M_\mathrm{eff}(t)$ fluctuates. The image you record is a time-average over a non-stationary process with variable $M$ — a fundamentally different statistical problem from the fixed-$M$ model the CRB assumes.

Mechanical drift. Confocal scanning over tens of seconds with galvo mirrors or piezo stages introduces drift of typically 1–5 nm per second. For a 98 s model-selection scan the accumulated drift is 100–500 nm — larger than the cluster itself. The effective PSF seen by the detector is the true PSF convolved with the drift trajectory, broadening it and destroying the position information the model relies on.

Background fluorescence. Bulk diamond surrounding the cluster contributes background photons that reduce the signal-to-noise ratio. The CRB with background $b$ (counts per pixel) is modified to $\sigma_x \sim \spsf/\sqrt{N_\mathrm{sig}^2/(N_\mathrm{sig}+N_\mathrm{bg})}$. For background-to-signal ratios of 0.5–5, typical in implanted diamond, the effective photon count is reduced by $2\times$–$10\times$, multiplying all the time estimates in Table 1 by the same factor.

What actually works. The experiments that have resolved multiple NV centers use one of three strategies, each of which sidesteps the barriers above: (1) spin-dependent contrast (single-spin STORM) — selectively driving individual NV spins with microwave pulses to switch emitters on and off one at a time, reducing $M_\mathrm{eff} = 1$ per frame and eliminating barriers 1–3 entirely; (2) deterministic PSF narrowing (STED, spin-RESOLFT) — shrinking $\seff$ until $d_\mathrm{min} > \seff$ so the cluster is resolved and the multi-emitter machinery never applies; (3) prior-assisted localization — using ion-beam implantation at known positions to supply a strong prior on $M^*$ and approximate emitter locations, converting the global optimization into a local refinement. All three approaches enforce $M_\mathrm{eff} \leq 1$ per measurement frame, which is the common thread identified in §7.

Beyond positions: combining with spin readout

This note has focused entirely on positional accuracy. For the NV experiment, the downstream goal is spin readout. The two problems have orthogonal failure modes: Rayleigh curse in the optical positioning step, and shot noise in the spin readout step. The optimal experiment combines them:

Once optical superresolution enforces $M_\mathrm{eff} \leq 1$, each NV occupies its own resolution element and its spin can be read out individually — no $K^2$ shot-noise penalty from combined emission. The cleanest protocol:

1. SR scan (ms timescale): localize all NVs in the field of view using STED or spin-RESOLFT. Store positions.

2. Spin readout (seconds timescale): hold beam fixed at each NV position; apply field-gradient + Ramsey sequence to read spins frequency-by-frequency.

Both steps benefit from the same enabling condition: $M_\mathrm{eff} \leq 1$. For a $5\times5$ lattice with mean occupancy $\bar n = 1$, total wall-clock time is $\sim 1$–$10$ s. A separate note covers the spin-readout SNR and gradient-ODMR scaling in detail.
