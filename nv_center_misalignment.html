§ 01

The NV Hamiltonian and Native Basis

The NV center is a spin-1 system. In the absence of any external magnetic field, the ground state Hamiltonian is simply the zero-field splitting term:

\( H_0 = D S_z^2 \) 1.1

The NV center has C\(_{3v}\) symmetry along its crystal axis (the N–V bond direction, which we call \(\hat{z}\)). The two unpaired electrons experience a spin-spin dipolar interaction. When you average this interaction over the orbital wavefunction, the only surviving term (by symmetry) is proportional to \(S_z^2\). This gives rise to \(D \approx 2.87\) GHz — the zero-field splitting between \(|m_s = 0\rangle\) and \(|m_s = \pm 1\rangle\).

Explicitly, \(S_z\) in the spin-1 basis \(\{|+1\rangle, |0\rangle, |-1\rangle\}\) is:

\( S_z = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix} \implies S_z^2 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \)

So \(H_0 = D S_z^2\) is already diagonal: \(|0\rangle\) has energy 0, and \(|\pm 1\rangle\) have energy \(D\). This is why \(\{|0\rangle, |+1\rangle, |-1\rangle\}\) are called the native eigenstates.

We call \(\{|0\rangle, |{+1}\rangle, |{-1}\rangle\}\) the native basis — these are the exact eigenstates of the NV crystal field Hamiltonian, quantized along the NV symmetry axis \(\hat{z}\).

When we add a perfectly aligned external field \(\vec{B} = B_z \hat{z}\), the full Hamiltonian becomes:

\( H = D S_z^2 + \gamma_e B_z S_z \) 1.2

The Zeeman term \(\gamma_e B_z S_z\) is proportional to \(S_z\), which is diagonal in the same native basis as \(S_z^2\). So adding it just shifts the eigenvalues — it does not mix the eigenstates at all.

The eigenvalues become: \(E_0 = 0\), \(E_{+1} = D + \gamma_e B_z\), \(E_{-1} = D - \gamma_e B_z\). The eigenvectors remain \(|0\rangle\), \(|+1\rangle\), \(|-1\rangle\) — identical to the zero-field case. This is why ODMR sees two peaks split by \(2\gamma_e B_z\) but the basis doesn't change.

Key Point
As long as \(\vec{B} \parallel \hat{z}\), the eigenstates remain the native basis states. The aligned field only shifts energies; it does not rotate the eigenstates.
§ 02

Effect of a Misaligned Magnetic Field

Now suppose \(\vec{B}\) has a transverse component: \(\vec{B} = B_z \hat{z} + B_\perp \hat{x}\). The Hamiltonian becomes:

\( H = D S_z^2 + \gamma_e B_z S_z + \gamma_e B_\perp S_x \) 2.1

For spin-1, the \(S_x\) matrix in the native basis is:

\( S_x = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \)

Unlike \(S_z\) and \(S_z^2\), \(S_x\) has off-diagonal elements — it connects \(|0\rangle \leftrightarrow |\pm 1\rangle\). This means any term \(\propto S_x\) in the Hamiltonian will mix the native basis states, making the native basis no longer the eigenbasis.

The matrix element \(\langle 0 | S_x | \pm 1 \rangle = 1/\sqrt{2}\) sets the scale of the mixing.

The transverse field introduces off-diagonal coupling in the native basis. The new eigenstates — found by diagonalizing the full Hamiltonian — are rotated versions of the native states. In the perturbative limit \(\gamma_e B_\perp \ll D\), defining \(\epsilon = \frac{\gamma_e B_\perp}{\sqrt{2}\, D}\), the new eigenstates are:

\( |\tilde{0}\rangle \approx |0\rangle - \epsilon\bigl(|{+1}\rangle + |{-1}\rangle\bigr) \) 2.2
\( |\widetilde{\pm 1}\rangle \approx |\pm 1\rangle + \epsilon\,|0\rangle \) 2.3

Treat \(H' = \gamma_e B_\perp S_x\) as a perturbation on \(H_0 = DS_z^2 + \gamma_e B_z S_z\). The unperturbed eigenstates are the native basis with energies \(E_0 = 0\), \(E_{\pm 1} = D \pm \gamma_e B_z\).

First-order perturbation theory gives the corrected eigenstates as:

\( |n^{(1)}\rangle = |n^{(0)}\rangle + \sum_{m \neq n} \frac{\langle m^{(0)} | H' | n^{(0)} \rangle}{E_n - E_m} |m^{(0)}\rangle \)

For \(|0\rangle\), we need the matrix elements \(\langle \pm 1 | \gamma_e B_\perp S_x | 0 \rangle = \gamma_e B_\perp / \sqrt{2}\), and the energy denominator is \(E_0 - E_{\pm 1} \approx -D\) (assuming \(\gamma_e B_z \ll D\)):

\( |\tilde{0}\rangle \approx |0\rangle + \frac{\gamma_e B_\perp/\sqrt{2}}{0 - D}(|+1\rangle + |-1\rangle) = |0\rangle - \epsilon(|+1\rangle + |-1\rangle) \)

where \(\epsilon = \gamma_e B_\perp / (\sqrt{2}\,D)\). Similarly for \(|\pm 1\rangle\): the coupling back to \(|0\rangle\) gives \(\epsilon|0\rangle\) admixture. Note \(|+1\rangle \leftrightarrow |-1\rangle\) coupling is second order (requires two \(S_x\) hops) and thus suppressed by \(\epsilon^2\).

The inverse relations — expressing the native states in the new eigenbasis — are:

\( |0\rangle \approx |\tilde{0}\rangle + \epsilon\bigl(|\widetilde{+1}\rangle + |\widetilde{-1}\rangle\bigr) \) 2.4
\( |\pm 1\rangle \approx |\widetilde{\pm 1}\rangle - \epsilon\,|\tilde{0}\rangle \) 2.5
§ 03

Green Initialization Under Misalignment

Green laser illumination initializes the NV spin via the spin-selective intersystem crossing (ISC): states \(|\pm 1\rangle\) decay preferentially through a non-radiative singlet pathway, which funnels population into \(|0\rangle\) after many optical cycles.

Critical Fact
The ISC mechanism is governed by the crystal field (C\(_{3v}\) symmetry), not the external magnetic field. Therefore, green light always initializes the native \(|0\rangle\), regardless of misalignment.

After green initialization, the prepared state in both bases is:

CaseNative BasisNew Eigenbasis
Aligned\(|0\rangle\)\(|\tilde{0}\rangle\)
Misaligned\(|0\rangle\)\(|\tilde{0}\rangle + \epsilon|\widetilde{+1}\rangle + \epsilon|\widetilde{-1}\rangle\)

We simply invert the transformation. Since the eigenstates form an orthonormal basis, we can expand \(|0\rangle\) as:

\( |0\rangle = \langle\tilde{0}|0\rangle|\tilde{0}\rangle + \langle\widetilde{+1}|0\rangle|\widetilde{+1}\rangle + \langle\widetilde{-1}|0\rangle|\widetilde{-1}\rangle \)

From eq. (2.2), \(\langle\tilde{0}|0\rangle \approx 1\) (to zeroth order in \(\epsilon\)). From eq. (2.3), \(\langle\widetilde{\pm 1}|0\rangle = \epsilon\) (the admixture of \(|0\rangle\) in \(|\widetilde{\pm 1}\rangle\)). So:

\( |0\rangle \approx |\tilde{0}\rangle + \epsilon|\widetilde{+1}\rangle + \epsilon|\widetilde{-1}\rangle \)

This confirms that the initialized state \(|0\rangle\), while trivial in the native basis, is a superposition of all three eigenstates. Since it is not an energy eigenstate, it will immediately begin to evolve under the Hamiltonian.

The consequence: in the misaligned case, the initialized state is not an eigenstate of the Hamiltonian. It will immediately begin to precess, even before any microwave pulse is applied. This is the first source of error.

§ 04

The Pi Pulse Under Misalignment

ODMR spectroscopy finds the resonance frequency by measuring fluorescence dips as a function of microwave frequency. It measures the actual energy gap between eigenstates — so it always calibrates to the new eigenbasis transition:

\( f_{ODMR} = \frac{E_{\widetilde{-1}} - E_{\tilde{0}}}{h} \) 4.1

A \(\pi\)-pulse at this frequency performs a complete population transfer \(|\tilde{0}\rangle \leftrightarrow |\widetilde{-1}\rangle\). Starting from the misaligned initialized state \(|\tilde{0}\rangle + \epsilon|\widetilde{+1}\rangle + \epsilon|\widetilde{-1}\rangle\), the \(\pi\)-pulse acts as:

\( |\tilde{0}\rangle + \epsilon|\widetilde{+1}\rangle + \epsilon|\widetilde{-1}\rangle \;\xrightarrow{\pi\text{-pulse}}\; |\widetilde{-1}\rangle + \epsilon|\widetilde{+1}\rangle + \epsilon|\widetilde{-1}\rangle \) 4.2

The \(\pi\)-pulse is resonant with the \(|\tilde{0}\rangle \leftrightarrow |\widetilde{-1}\rangle\) transition only. In the rotating wave approximation, it acts as a unitary within the \(\{|\tilde{0}\rangle, |\widetilde{-1}\rangle\}\) subspace:

\( U_\pi = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \quad \text{in the } \{|\tilde{0}\rangle, |\widetilde{-1}\rangle\} \text{ subspace} \)

The \(|\widetilde{+1}\rangle\) component is far off-resonant (its transition frequency differs by \(\sim 2D\)) so it is untouched. Therefore: \(|\tilde{0}\rangle \to |\widetilde{-1}\rangle\), \(|\widetilde{-1}\rangle \to |\tilde{0}\rangle\), \(|\widetilde{+1}\rangle \to |\widetilde{+1}\rangle\). Applying this to the input state gives eq. (4.2).

Collecting terms: \((1+\epsilon)|\widetilde{-1}\rangle + \epsilon|\widetilde{+1}\rangle\). Converting back to the native basis using eqs. (2.4) and (2.5):

\( \text{Final state} \approx |-1\rangle + \epsilon|0\rangle \) 4.3

Using \(|\widetilde{\pm 1}\rangle \approx |\pm 1\rangle + \epsilon|0\rangle\) from eq. (2.3):

\( (1+\epsilon)|\widetilde{-1}\rangle + \epsilon|\widetilde{+1}\rangle \approx (1+\epsilon)(|-1\rangle + \epsilon|0\rangle) + \epsilon(|+1\rangle + \epsilon|0\rangle) \)

Expanding and keeping terms to first order in \(\epsilon\):

\( \approx |-1\rangle + \epsilon|-1\rangle + \epsilon|0\rangle + \epsilon|+1\rangle + \mathcal{O}(\epsilon^2) \)

The \(\epsilon|-1\rangle\) term is a small correction to the amplitude of \(|-1\rangle\) (a normalization effect), and \(\epsilon|+1\rangle\) is second-order small compared to the leading contamination. The dominant error is the \(\epsilon|0\rangle\) leakage, giving eq. (4.3).

Key Result
After the \(\pi\)-pulse, the state in the native basis is not a clean \(|-1\rangle\) — it has an \(\epsilon\)-order \(|0\rangle\) admixture. Since readout measures native \(|0\rangle\) population, this leakage directly contaminates the measurement.

§ 06

Ramsey Tomography and the Bloch Sphere

Standard Ramsey Readout

In the Ramsey scheme, two calibration measurements bracket the experiment:

ReferenceSequenceMeaning
Bright \(C_{bright}\)Green onlySystem in \(|0\rangle\) — max fluorescence
Dark \(C_{dark}\)Green + \(\pi\)-pulseSystem in \(|-1\rangle\) — min fluorescence

The inferred \(|0\rangle\) population from any measurement \(C_{meas}\) is:

\( P_0 = \frac{C_{meas} - C_{dark}}{C_{bright} - C_{dark}} \) 6.1

Full State Tomography

A single readout gives only \(\langle S_z \rangle\). To reconstruct the full Bloch vector, three projections are needed:

MeasurementPre-rotationExtracts
\(\langle S_z \rangle\)NoneDirect \(P_0\)
\(\langle S_x \rangle\)\(\pm Y/2\) pulseRotates X → Z axis
\(\langle S_y \rangle\)\(\pm X/2\) pulseRotates Y → Z axis

A \(+Y/2\) pulse rotates \(S_x \to S_z\), so the readout gives \(P_0^{+} \propto \langle S_z \rangle + \langle S_x \rangle + \text{offset}\). A \(-Y/2\) pulse gives \(P_0^{-} \propto \langle S_z \rangle - \langle S_x \rangle + \text{offset}\). Taking the difference:

\( P_0^{+} - P_0^{-} \propto 2\langle S_x \rangle \)

The common-mode \(\langle S_z \rangle\) term and any DC offset from laser drift or background fluorescence cancel exactly. This is why pairs of \(\pm\) pulses are used — they give a differential measurement that is immune to slow drifts.

Bloch Sphere Evolution: Aligned vs. Misaligned

For a state initialized to +X on the Bloch sphere (i.e., \(\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(|0\rangle + |-1\rangle)\)) with zero detuning:

ObservableAlignedMisaligned (native frame)
\(\langle S_x \rangle(t)\)\(e^{-t/T_2^*}\)Oscillates and decays
\(\langle S_y \rangle(t)\)\(0\)Non-zero — symmetry broken by tilt
\(\langle S_z \rangle(t)\)\(0\)Oscillates — leakage out of XY plane
Precession axisNative \(\hat{z}\)Tilted axis set by \(\vec{B}\)

In the new eigenbasis, the evolution is clean — the Bloch vector simply shrinks along +X with decay \((1-\epsilon^2)e^{-t/T_2^*}\). But since we can only measure in the native basis, the apparent dynamics look complicated and anomalous.

§ 07

Why Readout is Always in the Native Basis

The fluorescence rate operator is determined by the spin-orbit coupling in the NV excited state. The ISC rate from the excited triplet \(^3E\) to the singlet \(^1A_1\) is:

\( \Gamma_{ISC}^{(m_s)} \propto \bigl|\langle ^1A_1 | H_{SO} | ^3E,\, m_s\rangle\bigr|^2 \) 7.1

The spin-orbit coupling is \(H_{SO} = \lambda \vec{L} \cdot \vec{S}\). The crystal field imposes C\(_{3v}\) symmetry along the NV bond axis (native \(\hat{z}\)). By this symmetry, the orbital angular momentum \(\vec{L}\) is quantized along the same native \(\hat{z}\). So \(H_{SO}\) reduces to \(\lambda L_z S_z\) in the relevant subspace — which is diagonal in native \(m_s\).

This symmetry is an intrinsic property of the nitrogen-vacancy defect in diamond. It is set by the atomic positions — nitrogen, vacancy, and the surrounding carbon lattice — not by anything external. The external magnetic field \(\vec{B}\) enters only through the Zeeman term, which is far too small to disturb the crystal field or orbital structure.

Concretely: \(\Gamma_{ISC}^{(\pm 1)} \gg \Gamma_{ISC}^{(0)}\) because the \(|m_s = \pm 1\rangle\) states have nonzero matrix elements with \(H_{SO}\) while \(|m_s = 0\rangle\) does not (by C\(_{3v}\) symmetry selection rules).

The total fluorescence rate operator is therefore:

\( \hat{F} = \Gamma_0 |0\rangle\langle 0| + \Gamma_{\pm 1}\bigl(|{+1}\rangle\langle{+1}| + |{-1}\rangle\langle{-1}|\bigr) \) 7.2

This can be rewritten as a constant background plus a contrast term:

\( \hat{F} = \Gamma_{\pm 1}\,\mathbf{I} + (\Gamma_0 - \Gamma_{\pm 1})\,|0\rangle\langle 0| \) 7.3
Key Conclusion
All spin-state information in the fluorescence is carried by \(|0\rangle\langle 0|\) — a projector in the native basis. No external field can change this; it is written into the crystal symmetry.
§ 08

Why MW Pulses Always Address the Eigenbasis

The microwave Hamiltonian is:

\( H_{MW} = \Omega \cos(2\pi f_{MW}\, t)\, S_x \) 8.1

In the rotating wave approximation (RWA), this drive coherently couples states only when:

\( f_{MW} \approx \frac{E_m - E_n}{h} \) 8.2

In the interaction picture, the drive becomes \(H_{MW}^{(I)} \propto \Omega(e^{i(f_{MW} - f_{mn})t} + e^{-i(f_{MW} + f_{mn})t})\). When \(f_{MW} \neq f_{mn}\), both terms oscillate rapidly and average to zero over any timescale long compared to \(1/f_{mn}\). Only when \(f_{MW} \approx f_{mn}\) does the first term become nearly static and drive coherent Rabi oscillations. This is the RWA.

The energy levels \(E_n\) are the eigenvalues of the full Hamiltonian \(H = DS_z^2 + \gamma_e \vec{B}\cdot\vec{S}\). ODMR measures these gaps directly by scanning \(f_{MW}\) and observing fluorescence dips. So ODMR automatically finds the correct eigenbasis transition frequency — it has no knowledge of or preference for the native basis.

The Fundamental Mismatch
Initialization and readout are locked to the native basis by crystal physics. MW control is locked to the eigenbasis by resonance physics. No experimental choice can reconcile this — only removing the misalignment does.
§ 09

The PL Measurement Operator: Full Math

For a general state \(|\psi\rangle = \alpha|\tilde{0}\rangle + \beta|\widetilde{-1}\rangle + \gamma|\widetilde{+1}\rangle\) expressed in the eigenbasis, the measured PL is:

\( \text{PL} = \Gamma_{\pm 1} + (\Gamma_0 - \Gamma_{\pm 1})\,|\langle 0 | \psi\rangle|^2 \) 9.1

We need to evaluate \(\langle 0 | \psi \rangle\). Using the inverse transformation of eq. (2.4):

\( \langle 0 | = \langle \tilde{0}| + \epsilon\langle\widetilde{+1}| + \epsilon\langle\widetilde{-1}| \) 9.2

From eq. (2.4): \(|0\rangle \approx |\tilde{0}\rangle + \epsilon|\widetilde{+1}\rangle + \epsilon|\widetilde{-1}\rangle\). Taking the Hermitian conjugate (bra) of both sides gives eq. (9.2) directly, since all coefficients are real in our gauge.

Applying this to \(|\psi\rangle\) and using orthonormality of the eigenbasis:

\( \langle 0 | \psi\rangle = \alpha\langle\tilde{0}|\tilde{0}\rangle + \beta\langle\tilde{0}|\widetilde{-1}\rangle + \epsilon\gamma\langle\widetilde{+1}|\widetilde{+1}\rangle + \cdots = \alpha + \epsilon\gamma \) 9.3

Expanding \(\langle 0|\psi\rangle = (\langle\tilde{0}| + \epsilon\langle\widetilde{+1}| + \epsilon\langle\widetilde{-1}|)(\alpha|\tilde{0}\rangle + \beta|\widetilde{-1}\rangle + \gamma|\widetilde{+1}\rangle)\):

\( = \alpha\underbrace{\langle\tilde{0}|\tilde{0}\rangle}_{1} + \beta\underbrace{\langle\tilde{0}|\widetilde{-1}\rangle}_{0} + \gamma\underbrace{\langle\tilde{0}|\widetilde{+1}\rangle}_{0} + \epsilon\alpha\underbrace{\langle\widetilde{+1}|\tilde{0}\rangle}_{0} + \epsilon\beta\underbrace{\langle\widetilde{+1}|\widetilde{-1}\rangle}_{0} + \epsilon\gamma\underbrace{\langle\widetilde{+1}|\widetilde{+1}\rangle}_{1} + \cdots \)

By orthonormality of the eigenbasis, only the diagonal inner products survive: \(\langle\tilde{0}|\tilde{0}\rangle = 1\) and \(\langle\widetilde{+1}|\widetilde{+1}\rangle = 1\). The \(|\widetilde{-1}\rangle\) component \(\beta\) drops out entirely at first order — it only contributes at \(\mathcal{O}(\epsilon^2)\) through the \(\epsilon\langle\widetilde{-1}|\widetilde{-1}\rangle\) term, but this gives \(\epsilon\beta\) which when squared gives \(\epsilon^2|\beta|^2\). So the \(|-1\rangle\) population only leaks into PL at second order.

Therefore:

\( |\langle 0|\psi\rangle|^2 = |\alpha + \epsilon\gamma|^2 = |\alpha|^2 + 2\epsilon\,\text{Re}(\alpha^*\gamma) + \epsilon^2|\gamma|^2 \) 9.4
Full PL Expression
\[\text{PL} = \Gamma_{\pm 1} + (\Gamma_0 - \Gamma_{\pm 1})\Bigl[|\alpha|^2 + 2\epsilon\,\text{Re}(\alpha^*\gamma) + \mathcal{O}(\epsilon^2)\Bigr]\]

The three terms: (1) clean \(|\tilde{0}\rangle\) population you want, (2) first-order cross-contamination from \(|\widetilde{+1}\rangle\) leakage, (3) second-order corrections. Notably, the \(|\widetilde{-1}\rangle\) population \(|\beta|^2\) does not appear until \(\mathcal{O}(\epsilon^2)\).

§ 10

General Basis Transformation and Extreme Cases

The full transformation is a rotation of the quantization axis by angle \(\theta\) from native \(\hat{z}\) toward \(\hat{B}\). The mixing angle satisfies:

\( \tan(2\theta) = \frac{\gamma_e B_\perp}{D - \gamma_e B_z} \) 10.1

Consider the \(2\times 2\) subspace spanned by \(\{|0\rangle, |\text{sym}\rangle\}\) where \(|\text{sym}\rangle = (|+1\rangle + |-1\rangle)/\sqrt{2}\) (the state that couples to \(|0\rangle\) via \(S_x\)). In this subspace the Hamiltonian is:

\( H_{2\times 2} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & \gamma_e B_\perp/\sqrt{2} \\ \gamma_e B_\perp/\sqrt{2} & D - \gamma_e B_z \end{pmatrix} \)

This is a standard two-level avoided crossing. To diagonalize a \(2\times 2\) matrix \(\begin{pmatrix} a & c \\ c & b \end{pmatrix}\), the rotation angle satisfies \(\tan(2\theta) = 2c/(b-a)\). Substituting \(a=0\), \(b = D - \gamma_e B_z\), \(c = \gamma_e B_\perp/\sqrt{2}\):

\( \tan(2\theta) = \frac{2 \cdot \gamma_e B_\perp/\sqrt{2}}{D - \gamma_e B_z - 0} = \frac{\sqrt{2}\,\gamma_e B_\perp}{D - \gamma_e B_z} \)

The factor of \(\sqrt{2}\) absorbed into the definition of \(\epsilon\) gives the form in eq. (10.1). The eigenstates are then \(\cos\theta|0\rangle \pm \sin\theta|\text{sym}\rangle\).

The scale that controls the crossover between limits is set by \(D/\gamma_e \approx 102\) mT. Below this, the zero-field splitting protects the native basis; above it, the Zeeman energy dominates.

Condition Mixing Angle \(\theta\) Eigenbasis vs. Native Physical Regime
\(B = 0\) \(0\) Identical Zero field — \(D\) alone sets quantization
\(B_\perp = 0\), \(B_z \neq 0\) \(0\) Identical Perfectly aligned field
\(\gamma_e B_\perp \ll D\) \(\epsilon \ll 1\) Slightly rotated Small misalignment — our perturbative regime
\(\gamma_e B_\perp \sim D\) \(\sim \pi/4\) Maximally mixed Level anti-crossing (LAC)
\(\gamma_e B_\perp \gg D\) \(\pi/4\) Fully rotated to \(\hat{B}\) Zeeman dominates — \(D\) becomes perturbation

When \(\gamma_e B_\perp \gg D\), the denominator \(D - \gamma_e B_z\) is negligible compared to the numerator. So \(\tan(2\theta) \to \infty\), which means \(2\theta \to \pi/2\), giving \(\theta \to \pi/4\). This is the point of maximum mixing between \(|0\rangle\) and \(|\text{sym}\rangle\).

Physically, when the Zeeman energy greatly exceeds the zero-field splitting, the spin quantizes along \(\hat{B}\) rather than the NV axis. The NV's \(D\) term is now a small perturbation on top of the Zeeman Hamiltonian — the roles of \(D\) and \(\gamma_e B\) are swapped from the usual regime.

This is why high-field NV experiments (e.g., at tens of Tesla in pulsed magnets) behave qualitatively differently — the native basis intuition completely breaks down.

The Level Anti-Crossing
At \(\gamma_e B_\perp \sim D\) (around 102 mT of transverse field), the eigenstates are maximally hybridized. This is the NV transverse-field level anti-crossing (LAC). Near the LAC, the native basis is essentially useless as an intuition — the eigenstates are equal superpositions of native states. Initialization fidelity and readout contrast both degrade most severely here.
∎

The crossover field scale \(D/\gamma_e \approx 102\) mT sets the boundary between two distinct physical regimes. In nearly all tabletop NV experiments, \(\gamma_e B \ll D\), so the perturbative treatment here is quantitatively accurate.
